## 1. Core Principles of Mediation <a name="core-principles"></a>

Mediation is negotiation between disputing parties assisted by an impartial neutral third party who has no decision-making power. The key characteristics are:

**Voluntary:** No party is forced to participate or to accept any particular settlement. The decision to mediate and the terms of any agreement rest entirely with the parties.

**Non-Coercive:** The mediator facilitates but does not decide. The mediator helps parties make their own decisions rather than imposing outcomes.

**Assisted Negotiation:** The mediator provides procedural assistance, helps build relationships, and may suggest substantive options — but the parties retain ownership of the outcome.

**Informal:** Unlike courts or arbitration, mediation has no prescribed rules of procedure. The parties design the process to suit their needs.

**Confidential:** Mediation is generally confidential, with limits jointly established by the parties. Confidentiality terms should be formalized in writing. Note: specific legislative requirements (e.g., access to information laws, privacy acts) may affect the scope of confidentiality, particularly when government entities are involved.

**Resolution-Oriented:** The goal is to move beyond fault-based legal analysis toward understanding each party's actual needs and interests, enabling outcomes that truly resolve the underlying problems.

## 2. The 12 Stages of Mediation <a name="twelve-stages"></a>

While every mediation varies based on complexity, personalities, and the degree to which positions have hardened, successful mediations typically involve these stages:

### Pre-Mediation Phase

**Stage 1: Initial Contact with Disputing Parties**
- Build credibility with both parties
- Build rapport and trust
- Educate parties about the mediation process
- Determine whether legal counsel should be involved
- Increase commitment to the process

**Stage 2: Selecting a Strategy**
- Help parties evaluate various conflict resolution methods
- Assist in selecting the most appropriate approach for their specific situation

**Stage 3: Collecting and Analyzing Background Information**
- Gather and verify accurate data about the people involved, the context, and the substance of the dispute
- Review relevant documents, correspondence, and evidence

**Stage 4: Designing a Detailed Plan**
- Identify strategies that will enable parties to move toward agreement
- Plan the session structure (joint sessions, caucuses, sequencing)
- Address logistical requirements

### During Mediation

**Stage 5: Building Trust and Cooperation**
- Prepare parties to deal with difficult substantive issues
- Handle strong emotions constructively
- Identify and address stereotypes or misperceptions
- Build mutual recognition of legitimacy
- Clarify communication channels

**Stage 6: Beginning the Session**
- Open communication and negotiation between parties
- Establish an open and positive tone
- Set ground rules and behavioral guidelines
- Allow constructive venting of emotions

**Stage 7: Defining Issues and Setting an Agenda**
- Identify broad areas of concern
- Obtain agreement on which issues will be discussed
- Determine the sequence for addressing issues

**Stage 8: Uncovering Hidden Interests**
- Identify each party's substantive, procedural, and psychological interests
- Educate each party about the other's interests and needs
- Distinguish positions (what they say they want) from interests (why they want it)

**Stage 9: Generating Options for Settlement**
- Develop awareness of the need for multiple options
- Help parties move away from rigid positions
- Brainstorm creative solutions through dialogue

**Stage 10: Assessing Options**
- Review how each option addresses the parties' interests
- Assess costs and benefits of each option
- Apply reality testing against litigation alternatives (BATNA/WATNA)

### Closing Phase

**Stage 11: Final Bargaining**
- Bring parties' interests together
- Generate willingness to compromise
- Methods include: incremental convergence, package settlements, consensus formulas, or frameworks for further agreement

**Stage 12: Achieving Formal Settlement**
- Draft a Memorandum of Understanding or settlement agreement
- Identify procedural steps to implement the agreement
- Establish evaluation and monitoring procedures
- Create enforcement mechanisms

## 3. Role of the Mediator <a name="role-mediator"></a>

The mediator's role exists on a spectrum — from completely neutral and facilitative to more evaluative and directive. Regardless of approach, the mediator never has authority to impose a decision.

### Key Mediator Functions

**Facilitative:**
- Encourage information exchange between parties
- Help parties understand each other's perspectives
- Validate that each party's concerns are understood (active listening)
- Promote productive expression of emotions
- Lay out differences in perceptions and interests

**Procedural:**
- Identify and narrow the issues
- Suggest breaks when negotiations reach impasse
- Hold caucuses (separate meetings) with each party when needed
- Manage the sequence and pace of discussions

**Evaluative (when appropriate):**
- Help parties realistically evaluate alternatives to settlement
- Encourage flexibility and creativity
- Shift focus from past blame to future problem-solving
- Propose solutions that address fundamental interests of all parties

### Mediator Styles

| Style | Description | Best Used When |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Facilitative** | Focus on process; help parties find their own solution | Parties can communicate; relationship preservation matters |
| **Evaluative** | Provide opinion on merits; reality-test positions | Complex legal issues; parties need expert assessment |
| **Transformative** | Focus on empowerment and recognition | Relationship-heavy disputes; emotional conflicts |

## 4. Role of Counsel in Mediation <a name="role-counsel"></a>

Counsel's involvement should be determined at the outset. Options include:

**As Legal Advisor:** Present during mediation, advising client on legal rights, risks, and the implications of proposed settlements. Can review proposed agreements for legal adequacy.

**As Active Participant:** Speaks on behalf of client, presents arguments, negotiates directly. Useful when legal complexity is high or client is emotionally overwhelmed.

**As Representative:** In highly emotional cases, counsel may fully represent the client during sessions. This carries significant ethical obligations — counsel must communicate with clients, understand their perspective, and ensure the process addresses their needs.

**Not Present:** Counsel advises before and after, but is not at the table. Common in less formal mediations.

### Counsel Best Practices
- Prepare clients on how to present their perspective effectively
- Coach appropriate demeanor (facing the speaker, maintaining eye contact, keeping voice level, open posture)
- Monitor client reactions and suggest breaks when needed
- Help normalize feelings and save face after difficult moments
- Remind clients that impasse on one issue does not mean the mediation has failed
- Help clients understand the difference between their positions and their interests

## 5. Power Imbalance and Communication Difficulties <a name="power-imbalance"></a>

The mediator has a responsibility to ensure power imbalances do not compromise the mediation process.

### Identifying Power Imbalances
- Organizational hierarchy (employer vs. employee, senior vs. junior)
- Resource disparity (large corporation vs. individual)
- Gender, race, or cultural dynamics
- Knowledge asymmetry (one party has more information)
- Emotional vulnerability (harassment, discrimination cases)

### Mitigation Strategies

**Caucusing:** Separate meetings between the mediator and each party. Useful when:
- An impasse threatens resolution
- Communication has broken down
- One party is intimidated in joint sessions
- Sensitive information needs to be explored privately
- Emotions are running too high for productive dialogue

**Co-Mediation:** Two or more mediators equally involved in the process. Rationale:
- Helps parties who perceive themselves as weaker feel more comfortable
- Can address demographic representation (e.g., co-mediators of different genders in harassment cases)
- Provides more balanced process management
- Allows one mediator to observe dynamics while the other facilitates

**Ensuring Counsel Presence:** For the weaker party, having independent legal counsel present helps balance knowledge and advocacy.

**Process Adjustments:** Separate rooms, written exchanges, pre-mediation meetings with each party, allowing support persons.

## 6. Advantages and Limitations of Mediation <a name="advantages-limitations"></a>

### Advantages
- **Relationship preservation:** Consensual process avoids the adversarial elements that make continued relationships difficult after litigation
- **Flexibility:** No strict procedural rules; parties design a process suited to their needs
- **Confidentiality:** Private process (subject to applicable laws)
- **Speed and cost:** Generally faster and cheaper than litigation
- **Creative outcomes:** Not limited to legal remedies; can address interests that courts cannot
- **Party ownership:** Both parties define the terms, increasing compliance with the agreement
- **Complex disputes:** Particularly effective when multiple issues allow for trade-offs
- **Emotional management:** Neutral third party enables controlled dialogue when previous negotiations failed or emotions are high

### Limitations
- **Power imbalance risk:** Face-to-face meetings may disadvantage weaker parties (mitigated by caucusing, co-mediation, counsel)
- **No precedent:** Cannot establish legal precedent
- **Mediator influence:** A strong-willed mediator may exercise excessive control
- **Bad faith risk:** Non-binding nature may be exploited to delay resolution
- **Authority issues:** Institutional parties may face multi-level approval requirements
- **Not suitable for all disputes:** Cases requiring public accountability, legal precedent, or where one party is not acting in good faith may be better suited to other processes

## 7. Mediation Agreement Checklist <a name="agreement-checklist"></a>

Before beginning mediation, the following should be addressed:

### Participants
- All directly interested parties at the table (or represented)
- Additional participants identified (lawyers, experts, senior executives)
- Whether mediator can bring in additional parties if needed
- Representatives have sufficient authority to agree to a final resolution

### Process Design
- Mediator selection process agreed
- Fallback mechanism if parties cannot agree on a mediator
- Language/bilingual requirements considered
- Mediation agreement executed, covering:
  - Mediator's mandate and scope
  - Provision for written agreement if dispute is resolved
  - Confidentiality terms
  - Mediator's remuneration
  - Responsibilities of mediator and parties
  - Equal cost-sharing arrangement

### Logistics
- Date, time, and duration limits set
- Neutral location chosen
- Special requirements met (translation, accessibility, technology)
- Procedure established and agreed upon (or authority delegated to mediator)

### Disclosure
- Disclosure process established (advance exchange or as required by mediator)
- Key documents identified and shared

### Post-Mediation
- Any agreement subject to independent legal advice?
- Who will draft the agreement/MOU?
- Fallback process articulated if mediation is unsuccessful (arbitration, litigation, further negotiation)
