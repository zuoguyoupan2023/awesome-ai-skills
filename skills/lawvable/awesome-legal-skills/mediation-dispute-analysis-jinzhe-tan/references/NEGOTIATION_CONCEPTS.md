## 1. Positions vs. Interests <a name="positions-interests"></a>

The single most important distinction in mediation analysis.

**Position:** What a party says they want. A stated demand or offer.
- "I want $500,000 in damages."
- "I refuse to pay anything."

**Interest:** Why a party wants it. The underlying need, concern, or motivation.
- "I need to cover the costs of finding a new supplier and the revenue I lost during the transition."
- "I believe we performed the contract properly and paying would set a bad precedent for future clients."

### Types of Interests

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Substantive** | Tangible outcomes — money, property, specific actions | Payment, delivery, contract performance, access to resources |
| **Procedural** | How the process works — fairness, voice, transparency | Being heard, fair process, timely resolution, having input into the outcome |
| **Psychological** | Emotional and relational needs | Recognition, respect, apology, saving face, sense of justice, closure |
| **Relationship** | Preservation or restructuring of the ongoing relationship | Continued business, reputation protection, future cooperation |

### Why This Matters for Mediation

Courts address positions (who is legally right). Mediation addresses interests (what each party actually needs). A good mediation analysis identifies interests that can be satisfied through creative means the court system cannot provide — apologies, restructured agreements, future business opportunities, process changes.

## 2. BATNA / WATNA Analysis <a name="batna-watna"></a>

### BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement

The best outcome a party can realistically achieve if mediation fails and they pursue their next-best option (usually litigation).

**How to assess BATNA:**
1. What is the most likely alternative? (Usually litigation, sometimes arbitration or doing nothing)
2. What is the probable outcome of that alternative?
3. How long will it take?
4. What will it cost (legal fees, management time, opportunity costs)?
5. What are the non-monetary consequences (publicity, relationship damage, precedent)?

**Example:**
> Party A's BATNA: Proceed to trial. Based on the strength of their contract claim, they have a ~65% chance of winning $300,000, but trial would take 18 months and cost $80,000 in legal fees. Expected value: (0.65 × $300,000) - $80,000 = $115,000. Additionally, the litigation would likely end the business relationship permanently.

### WATNA — Worst Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement

The worst realistic outcome if mediation fails — not the absolute worst-case scenario, but a plausible bad outcome.

**How to assess WATNA:**
1. What if the case goes to trial and the party loses?
2. What are the maximum costs (including adverse cost awards)?
3. What are the worst non-monetary consequences?
4. Factor in counterclaims or unexpected developments.

### Using BATNA/WATNA in Mediation Strategy

The gap between BATNA and WATNA represents a party's **risk range.** Parties with:
- **Strong BATNA, mild WATNA:** Less incentive to settle; will hold firm
- **Weak BATNA, severe WATNA:** More incentive to settle; more flexible
- **Both parties with significant WATNA risk:** Strong incentive for both to find agreement

The mediator (and counsel) should help parties realistically assess their alternatives, countering both overconfidence and excessive pessimism.

## 3. Zone of Possible Agreement (ZOPA) <a name="zopa"></a>

The ZOPA is the range where both parties' acceptable outcomes overlap. If no ZOPA exists, settlement is unlikely unless interests can be reframed or expanded.

### Identifying the ZOPA

**For monetary disputes:**
- Party A's reservation point: The minimum they will accept (informed by their BATNA)
- Party B's reservation point: The maximum they will pay (informed by their BATNA)
- ZOPA exists if: Party B's maximum > Party A's minimum

**Example:**
> Party A will not accept less than $150,000 (their expected litigation value minus costs and risk).
> Party B will not pay more than $220,000 (their expected litigation exposure plus legal costs).
> ZOPA: $150,000 – $220,000

**For non-monetary disputes:**
ZOPA is harder to define numerically but exists when both parties can accept an outcome. Look for:
- Overlapping interests that can be addressed simultaneously
- Trade-offs across issues (I give on X if you give on Y)
- Creative solutions that expand the total value available

### When No ZOPA Exists

If there appears to be no ZOPA:
1. **Re-examine assumptions:** Are parties' assessments of litigation risk realistic?
2. **Expand the pie:** Can non-monetary interests be addressed to make the overall package acceptable?
3. **Introduce new information:** Would a neutral expert opinion, appraisal, or legal assessment change perceptions?
4. **Reframe the issue:** Can the dispute be reconceptualized in a way that creates new options?
5. **Address barriers:** Sometimes emotional factors (anger, desire for vindication) inflate reservation points beyond rational levels.

## 4. Interest-Based Negotiation Framework <a name="interest-based"></a>

Based on the Harvard Negotiation Project principles (Fisher & Ury, "Getting to Yes"), adapted for mediation preparation:

### Core Principles

**Separate the People from the Problem**
- Acknowledge emotions without letting them drive the analysis
- Distinguish between the human relationship and the substantive issues
- Address perception gaps and communication problems as separate issues

**Focus on Interests, Not Positions**
- Ask "why?" behind every stated position
- Look for interests that can be satisfied in multiple ways
- Identify shared interests as building blocks for agreement

**Invent Options for Mutual Gain**
- Brainstorm before evaluating
- Look for creative solutions that address both parties' key interests
- Consider trade-offs across different issues
- Think beyond pure monetary resolution

**Use Objective Criteria**
- Where possible, anchor proposals in independent standards (market rates, industry norms, legal precedent, expert valuations)
- Objective criteria help parties accept outcomes without feeling they "lost"

### Interest Mapping Template

For each party, map:

```
STATED POSITION:    "I want X."
   ↓ WHY?
PRIMARY INTEREST:   "Because I need Y."
   ↓ WHY?
DEEPER INTEREST:    "Because Y protects Z."
   ↓ WHY?
CORE NEED:          "Because Z is essential to my [security/identity/livelihood]."
```

The deeper you go, the more options emerge for satisfying the underlying need.

## 5. Settlement Option Patterns <a name="settlement-patterns"></a>

Common settlement structures that work well in mediation:

### Financial Settlements

| Pattern | When to Use | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Lump sum** | Clean break needed; amount is clear | One-time payment of $X |
| **Structured payment** | Cash flow constraints; ongoing relationship | Monthly payments over 12 months |
| **Risk-adjusted** | Liability uncertain; both sides see risk | Discounted amount reflecting litigation odds |
| **Contingent** | Outcome depends on future events | Payment tied to future revenue or project success |

### Non-Financial Elements

| Element | When to Use |
|---------|-------------|
| **Apology / Acknowledgment** | Emotional harm; party needs recognition |
| **Process changes** | Systemic issue caused the dispute; prevents recurrence |
| **Continued relationship** | Both parties benefit from ongoing dealings |
| **Confidentiality clause** | Reputational concerns for one or both parties |
| **Reference / recommendation** | Employment disputes; party needs to move forward |
| **Training / education** | Harassment, discrimination; institutional improvement |
| **Future business terms** | Commercial relationship worth preserving |
| **Public statement / retraction** | Defamation, reputational harm |

### Package Deals

Combine multiple elements to create value. The key principle: parties may value different issues differently, allowing trade-offs where both gain.

**Example:** In a commercial dispute, Party A cares most about money, Party B cares most about the ongoing relationship. A settlement combining a moderate payment (less than A's maximum demand) with a renewed contract (valuable to B and providing A future revenue) can make both parties better off than a pure monetary settlement.

## 6. Impasse-Breaking Techniques <a name="impasse"></a>

When negotiation stalls, the mediator and counsel have several tools:

### Process Techniques
- **Take a break:** Allow emotions to cool and parties to reconsider
- **Caucus:** Private meetings to explore flexibility each party won't show in joint session
- **Change the sequence:** Move to a different issue and return later
- **Adjourn and reconvene:** Allow time for reflection, consultation with advisors, or gathering additional information

### Reframing Techniques
- **Shift from past to future:** "Instead of who caused this, what would a good outcome look like going forward?"
- **Shift from blame to problem-solving:** "How can we prevent this from happening again?"
- **Normalize the difficulty:** "It's common for negotiations to reach a difficult point — it doesn't mean we can't find a solution"
- **Acknowledge underlying interests:** "It sounds like what's really important to you is [interest], not just [position]"

### Analytical Techniques
- **Reality testing:** "If this goes to court, what do you think will happen? What will it cost?"
- **One-text procedure:** Mediator drafts a single proposal that both parties critique (instead of adversarial counter-offers)
- **Contingent agreement:** "What if we agreed that X happens only if Y occurs?"
- **Neutral expert opinion:** Bring in a third-party expert to assess a technical or valuation question

### Structural Techniques
- **Expand the pie:** Add issues or resources that create new value
- **Log-rolling:** Trade concessions on less important issues for gains on more important ones
- **Bridging:** Find a new option that satisfies both parties' primary interests without either giving up their core need
- **Post-settlement settlement:** Reach a basic agreement, then negotiate improvements that make both parties better off
