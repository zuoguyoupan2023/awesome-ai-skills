# Albers — Interaction of Color

**Source:** _Interaction of Color: text of the original edition with revised plate section_
**Author:** Josef Albers
**Archive.org:** [archive.org/details/interactionofcol0000albe](https://archive.org/details/interactionofcol0000albe)

## What It Is

One of the classic teaching texts on **color relativity**: simultaneous contrast, perceptual shifts, and the fact that colors are seen in relationship, not in isolation.

## Key Points

- **Color is relational.** The same swatch can appear different depending on context.
- **Perception beats formula.** Albers teaches through exercises and comparisons, not through one rigid theory system.
- **Simultaneous contrast is practical, not abstract.** Designers and painters hit these effects constantly.
- **Pedagogically important, scientifically incomplete.** It is invaluable for training perception, but it is not a substitute for modern color science.

## Why This Matters for the Skill

- Useful when a user needs help with **visual interaction**, **contrast illusions**, or **perception-first exercises**.
- Strong complement to more scientific references: Albers is excellent for **seeing** color effects, Fairchild/Hunt for **explaining** them.
- Also a useful historical counterweight to Itten-style formula teaching.

## Practical Use

- Use for **simultaneous contrast**, **teaching exercises**, and context-dependent design decisions.
- Use when explaining why a color system needs **testing in context**, not just token values in isolation.
