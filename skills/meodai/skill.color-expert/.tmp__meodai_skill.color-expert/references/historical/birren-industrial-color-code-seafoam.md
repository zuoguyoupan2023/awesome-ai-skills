# Why So Many Control Rooms Were Seafoam Green — Faber Birren's Industrial Color Code

**Source:** [Beth Mathews Design](https://bethmathews.substack.com) (Substack)
**Author:** Beth Mathews
**Date:** 2025-12-17
**URL:** https://bethmathews.substack.com/p/why-so-many-control-rooms-were-seafoam
**Subtitle:** The Color Theory Behind Industrial Seafoam Green

## Description

A designer's investigation into the specific seafoam/mint green found throughout mid-20th century American industrial facilities — Manhattan Project control rooms, factories, schools, hospitals — traced back to Faber Birren's functional color code, approved by the National Safety Council in 1944 and mandatory since 1948. The piece centers on Birren's _Color for Interiors: Historical and Modern_ (1963), Chart 13 / Chapter 16 "Industrial Plants," which pairs eight soft wall tones with eight brilliant safety colors.

The article is the clearest short-form explanation of _why_ a very specific blue-green dominates these spaces: it's not style, it's brightness control in the field of view to reduce eye fatigue in environments where a perceptual error could kill people. The soft greens are paired with a disciplined safety-code palette (fire red, solar yellow, alert orange, safety green, caution blue) used sparingly, only for hazards.

## Article Content

### The Tour

In the summer of 2017, Mathews visited the Manhattan Project National Historical Park at Oak Ridge, TN. Oak Ridge ("Site X") grew from 3,000 people in 1942 to 75,000 by 1945 as part of the secret plutonium/uranium enrichment effort.

The first stop was the **X-10 Graphite Reactor** — a 24-foot-square graphite block, the world's second full-scale nuclear reactor. Plutonium produced here was shipped to Los Alamos for research into the _Fat Man_ atomic bomb. What caught her eye as a designer, besides the knobs, levers, and buttons, was the use of **a very specific seafoam green** on the reactor's walls and in the control panel room.

### Faber Birren

In the fall of 1919, Faber Birren entered the Art Institute at the University of Chicago, only to drop out in the spring of 1921 to commit himself to self-education in color — such a program didn't exist. He spent his days interviewing psychologists and physicists and conducted his own color studies, which were considered unconventional at the time. He painted his bedroom walls red vermillion to test if it would make him go mad.

In 1933, he moved to New York City and became a self-appointed color consultant, approaching major corporations to sell the idea that appropriate use of color could boost sales. He convinced a Chicago wholesale meat company that the company's white walls made the meat unappealing; he studied the steaks on various colored backgrounds and determined that a blue/green background would make the beef appear redder. Sales went up, and soon a number of industries hired Birren to bring color theory into their work, including the leading chemical and wartime contract company — and Manhattan Project building designer — **DuPont**.

### Industrial Color Coding

With the increase in wartime production in the US during WWII, Birren and DuPont created a master color safety code for the industrial plant industry, with the aim of reducing accidents and increasing efficiency within plants. These color codes were **approved by the National Safety Council in 1944** and are now internationally recognized, having been **mandatory practice since 1948**. The coding went as such:

- **Fire Red** — all fire protection, emergency stop buttons, and flammable liquids
- **Solar Yellow** — caution and physical hazards such as falling
- **Alert Orange** — hazardous parts of machinery
- **Safety Green** — safety features such as first-aid equipment, emergency exits, eyewash stations
- **Caution Blue** — non-safety information, notices, or out-of-order signage
- **Light Green** — used on walls to reduce visual fatigue

### Chart 13, Chapter 16 — Industrial Plants (Birren 1963)

The article reproduces _Chart 13 for Chapter 16_ from _Color for Interiors: Historical and Modern_. It is a two-column palette: the left column is soft, desaturated wall colors; the right column is brilliant, saturated safety-code colors used sparingly to mark hazards.

| Soft (wall / field-of-view) | Brilliant (safety code / hazards) |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Light Green                 | Medium Green                      |
| Beige                       | Sandalwood                        |
| Light Blue                  | Medium Blue                       |
| Soft Yellow                 | Solar Yellow                      |
| Light Gray                  | Alert Orange                      |
| Medium Gray                 | Fire Red                          |
| Deep Gray                   | Safety Green                      |
| Spotlight Buff              | Caution Blue                      |

Birren's caption on the chart itself:

> "The importance of color in factories is first to control brightness in the general field of view for an efficient seeing condition. Interiors can then be conditioned for emotional pleasure and interest, using warm, cool, or luminous hues as working conditions suggest. Color should be functional and not merely decorative. The brilliant samples in the second column of the chart are for use as a safety code to mark important hazards."

And on why the standards are soft in tone:

> "Note that most of the standards are soft in tone. This is deliberate and intended to establish a non-distracting environment. Green is a restful and natural-looking color for average factory interiors. Light Green with Medium Green is suggested."

### The Hanford Site — B Reactor

Mathews researched the second Manhattan Project plant, the Hanford Site — home to the **B Reactor**, the first full-scale plutonium production reactor in the world. The site looks like an ode to Birren's light green and color codes, which makes sense since DuPont was also responsible for the design and construction of Hanford.

Applied to the B-Reactor control room (per Birren's prescription for small industrial areas):

- ✓ **Medium Green for a dado** (lower part of wall, waist height)
- ✓ **Medium Gray** for machinery, equipment, and racks
- ✓ **Fire Red** reserved exclusively for fire protection devices
- ✓ **Beige walls** for interiors deprived of natural light
- ✓ **Light-color floor**

> "As we can see, his color theory was followed to a T."

### Other US Industrial Plants That Used These Color Methods

The same palette language shows up in: the **US Pipe Factory**, the **Scranton Lace Factory**, and Christopher Payne's _Textile Series_ documenting surviving American mills. The convention persisted into hospitals (1990s), schools, and mental health institutions (1970s — where reader comments also recall Baker-Miller Pink experiments for calming effect).

### Germany's Counterpart

Mathews flags a future topic: **Cologne Bridge Green** (_Kölnbrücken-Grün_) — a specific seafoam shade Germany developed for bridge infrastructure. Distinct lineage, similar aesthetic result.

## Key Concepts

- **Functional color, not decorative color** — Birren's central thesis. Interior color in a factory/control room is an engineering variable (brightness control, fatigue reduction, accident reduction, error rates), not a style choice. Decoration is secondary and optional.
- **Brightness control in the general field of view** — soft, medium-value, medium-chroma greens/beiges dominate surface area so the eye isn't fighting large bright or large dark zones. This is why walls are _light green_ and machinery is _medium gray_: they sit near the same mid-value band.
- **Sparing brilliant color = information channel** — the brilliant column is reserved for coded information (fire, caution, hazard, first-aid). Because soft tones dominate the background, saturated color stands out as signal rather than noise. This is an early _semantic color system_: brilliant hues are not just decoration but a dedicated information layer.
- **The dado convention** — medium green on the lower wall (to waist height), lighter above. Practical for durability (dirt/marks hide in the darker value at hand/foot level) and perceptually grounds the space.
- **Light floors in low-light interiors** — reflectance amplifies available light; beige walls perform the same job when natural light is absent.
- **The 1944 NSC / 1948-mandatory codes** — the "seafoam green control room" aesthetic is not mid-century nostalgia but a compliance artifact. When people now call this look "institutional," they are identifying the residue of a safety standard.
- **Integrated vs. isolated** — Birren's system is not just a palette, it's a decision graph: field-of-view color follows from fatigue research; hazard color follows from information coding; specific application (dado, racks, floor, fire box) follows from task. Modern semantic design-token systems parallel this structure directly (reference palette → semantic roles → component usage).
- **Blue-green as appetite-redder (the meat-counter origin)** — Birren's earlier discovery that a blue/green background makes red meat appear redder is a simultaneous-contrast / chromatic adaptation effect. It's the same color-psychology instinct that later informed his factory-green recommendation: greens aren't just "restful," they bias red perception (making skin, blood, warning indicators, and emergency buttons register more vividly against them).
