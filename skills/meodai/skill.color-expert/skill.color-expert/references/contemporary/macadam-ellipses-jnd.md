# MacAdam Ellipses — Just Noticeable Color Difference

**Primary Source:** David L. MacAdam, “Visual sensitivities to color differences in daylight” (JOSA, 1942)
**DOI:** [doi.org/10.1364/JOSA.32.000247](https://doi.org/10.1364/JOSA.32.000247)
**Overview:** [en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacAdam_ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacAdam_ellipse)

## What It Is

MacAdam ellipses are the classic demonstration that **equal geometric distances in CIE 1931 xy are not equal perceptual differences**. They describe regions of color that are effectively indistinguishable around a reference point.

## Key Points

- **Perceptual sensitivity is uneven.** Ellipse size and orientation change across the chromaticity diagram.
- **The xy diagram is not perceptually uniform.** Some areas support much finer discrimination than others.
- **JNDs are local, not global.** A single “distance threshold” does not behave consistently in non-uniform spaces.
- **LED binning still uses this language.** “3-step MacAdam” is common industry shorthand for perceptual consistency.

## Why This Matters for the Skill

- Strong corrective to naive answers that treat **xy distance** or **HSL distance** as perceptual truth.
- Useful for explaining why **Lab, Luv, CAM16-UCS, and OKLAB** exist at all.
- Good evidence when arguing that **perceptual spaces are approximations of discrimination**, not arbitrary trends.

## Practical Use

- Use when comparing **distance formulas** or **uniform color spaces**.
- Use when explaining **JNDs**, tolerance bands, or LED binning language.
- Use as a short, canonical answer to “why isn’t the chromaticity diagram uniform?”
