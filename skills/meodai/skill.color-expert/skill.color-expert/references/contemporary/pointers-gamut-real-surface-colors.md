# Pointer's Gamut — Real Surface Colors

**Primary Context:** Michael R. Pointer's real-surface gamut work (1980)
**Overview:** [en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamut#Pointer's_gamut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamut#Pointer's_gamut)
**Related Paper:** Luo, Pointer, Green — _A Spectral Based Colour Gamut for Real Objects_ (ICC/CIE workshop PDF)

## What It Is

Pointer's gamut is a practical reference gamut for **real diffusely reflecting surfaces**. It is smaller than the theoretical optimal color solid and much more useful when talking about colors that actual objects, paints, papers, and textiles can have.

## Key Points

- **Real surfaces do not reach theoretical maxima.** The optimal color solid is a limit case; ordinary objects sit inside it.
- **Pointer's gamut is empirical.** It was derived from thousands of measured real surface samples.
- **Diffuse reflection matters.** Glossy or specular materials can fall outside Pointer's gamut.
- **Surface color and display color are different domains.** A display can express chromaticities that are poor models of object colors.

## Why This Matters for the Skill

- Useful corrective when people assume **all mathematically valid colors are realistic object colors**.
- Helps explain why **paint naming systems** and **surface-color dictionaries** differ from RGB-grid naming systems.
- Good foundation for discussing **gamut realism**, **surface color references**, and **object-color plausibility**.

## Practical Use

- Use when discussing **real object colors** rather than emissive display colors.
- Use when evaluating **paint datasets**, **surface naming systems**, or **print gamuts**.
- Pair with Jaffer, Ridgway, and Munsell references for surface-color workflows.
