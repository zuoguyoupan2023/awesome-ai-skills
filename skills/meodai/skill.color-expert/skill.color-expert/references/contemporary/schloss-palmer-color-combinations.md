# Aesthetic Response to Color Combinations

**Source:** Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics 73 (2011)
**Authors:** Karen B. Schloss, Stephen E. Palmer
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3758/s13414-010-0027-0
**License:** Open access via Springer
**Local PDF:** [pdfs/13414_2010_Article_27.pdf](pdfs/13414_2010_Article_27.pdf)

## Problem

The color harmony literature often talks past itself because it mixes together three different judgments:

- liking a color pair as a whole
- judging the pair as harmonious
- liking one color as a figure against a colored background

Schloss and Palmer argue that many old contradictions come from treating those as if they were the same question.

## Core Distinction

The paper separates three measures:

1. **Pair preference** - do people like the two colors together?
2. **Harmony judgment** - does the pair feel visually coherent?
3. **Figural color preference** - does the foreground color become more appealing on that background?

That framing matters because each judgment responds differently to hue similarity, contrast, and component-color liking.

## Key Findings

- **Pair preference and harmony both increase with hue similarity.** Similar-hue pairs are generally rated as more harmonious and more likable as a pair.
- **Lightness contrast still matters.** People do not only respond to hue relation; pair preference is also shaped by how much they already like the component colors and by lightness contrast between them.
- **Foreground preference works differently.** A figural color can become more appealing when placed against a hue-contrasting background, even when that same high-contrast pair is not judged especially harmonious.
- **The old "similar vs complementary" argument is partly a category error.** Different tasks produce different winners.

## Why This Matters

- Useful corrective for simplistic harmony rules like "complements are always best" or "similar hues are always boring."
- Supports the skill's broader position that **context changes color judgments**.
- Helps explain why UI/background pairings, posters, and data-visualization accents can benefit from contrast even when the full composition is not perceived as harmonious.

## Practical Takeaways

- Use **hue similarity** when the goal is calmness or overall pair harmony.
- Use **hue contrast** when the goal is to make a foreground color pop against a background.
- Evaluate palette decisions based on the actual task: pair mood, legibility, emphasis, or figure-ground separation.
