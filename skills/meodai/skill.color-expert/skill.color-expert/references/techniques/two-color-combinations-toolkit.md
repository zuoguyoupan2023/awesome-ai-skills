## Core Idea

Duru proposes a practical way to judge two-color pairings by the **amount of stimulation** they create rather than by relying only on named harmony formulas.

The goal is to avoid both extremes:

- pairings that are overexciting
- pairings that are inert or sleepy

The most satisfying combinations often sit in the middle.

## Why It Works

This framing is useful because two-color compositions are sensitive to more than hue relationship alone. The perceived effect depends on:

- contrast level
- proportion
- saturation intensity
- the overall information load of the pair

That makes "stimulation" a better practical lens than a rigid complementary/analogous label.

## Key Takeaways

- Minimal color systems can still cover a wide emotional range.
- Attractive pairs often avoid both maximal clash and total sameness.
- Intentional pairing benefits from repeated study, iteration, and comparison rather than one-off rule following.

## Why It Matters for the Skill

- Good companion to the repo's skepticism about simplistic harmony rules
- Useful for posters, identities, editorial design, and small palette systems
- Connects well to character-first thinking: the pair's felt energy often matters more than its textbook hue interval

## Practical Takeaways

- Judge pairs by how much stimulation they create, not only by wheel geometry.
- For small systems, two-color studies can expose useful constraints before scaling to larger palettes.
- A strong pair often comes from calibrated imbalance, not equal visual force.
