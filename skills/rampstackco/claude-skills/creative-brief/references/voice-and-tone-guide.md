# Voice and Tone Guide

Voice is who you are. Tone is how you sound right now. Voice stays constant. Tone shifts with context (a 404 page sounds different from an onboarding email, even if the voice is the same).

This guide gives you frameworks for picking a voice and adapting tone, plus worked examples.

---

## Framework 1: Four dimensions (Nielsen Norman)

Pick a position on each axis. Together they describe the voice.

| Dimension | One end | Other end |
|---|---|---|
| Formality | Formal | Casual |
| Seriousness | Serious | Funny |
| Respectfulness | Respectful | Irreverent |
| Enthusiasm | Matter-of-fact | Enthusiastic |

A bank: formal, serious, respectful, matter-of-fact.
A craft soda brand: casual, funny, irreverent, enthusiastic.
A premium watch brand: formal, serious, respectful, matter-of-fact.
A coding bootcamp: casual, serious, respectful, enthusiastic.

If you cannot pick a position on each axis, the voice is not defined yet.

---

## Framework 2: Brand archetypes

Twelve archetypes from Jung-derived brand theory. Pick one primary and one secondary.

| Archetype | Promise | Voice notes | Example brands |
|---|---|---|---|
| Innocent | Safety, simplicity | Warm, plain, reassuring | Dove, Coca-Cola |
| Sage | Truth, wisdom | Authoritative, considered, measured | Google, BBC, The Economist |
| Explorer | Freedom, discovery | Adventurous, restless, vivid | Patagonia, Jeep, Subaru |
| Outlaw | Disruption | Rebellious, blunt, anti-establishment | Harley-Davidson, Liquid Death |
| Magician | Transformation | Visionary, mysterious, evocative | Apple, Tesla |
| Hero | Mastery, courage | Bold, confident, action-driving | Nike, FedEx |
| Lover | Intimacy, beauty | Sensual, warm, indulgent | Chanel, Godiva |
| Jester | Joy, fun | Playful, irreverent, witty | Old Spice, Mailchimp, Slack |
| Everyman | Belonging | Friendly, plain, relatable | IKEA, Target |
| Caregiver | Service, compassion | Nurturing, warm, supportive | Johnson & Johnson, Volvo |
| Ruler | Control, prestige | Commanding, polished, exclusive | Rolex, Mercedes |
| Creator | Self-expression | Imaginative, articulate, craft-focused | Lego, Adobe |

Two archetypes give you range. A primary "Sage" with secondary "Hero" is authoritative AND action-driving. A primary "Caregiver" with secondary "Sage" is supportive AND wise.

---

## Framework 3: We are X, not Y

The fastest voice exercise. Pick three to five adjectives. For each, name what it could tip into if overdone.

> **Confident**, not arrogant.
> **Direct**, not blunt.
> **Warm**, not saccharine.
> **Witty**, not sarcastic.
> **Smart**, not condescending.

The "not" half is what saves you. "Confident" alone leads writers to swagger. "Confident, not arrogant" tells them where the line is.

## Tone shifts by context

Voice stays. Tone shifts. Map the high-frequency moments and decide how each one sounds.

| Moment | Tone shift |
|---|---|
| Onboarding | Warmer, more enthusiastic |
| Error message | Calmer, more matter-of-fact |
| Success state | Brief celebration, quickly redirect to next action |
| Empty state | Helpful, slightly playful, suggests action |
| 404 / not found | Self-aware, light, points the way home |
| Account deletion | Quiet, respectful, no jokes |
| Pricing page | Direct, clear, confidence-inspiring |
| Legal copy | Plain language version of the legal copy, sitting next to the legal copy |

## Practical patterns

Voice shows up in concrete choices. These are the dials.

**Sentence length**

- Short sentences = punchy, confident. (Apple, Stripe, Liquid Death.)
- Medium sentences = considered, professional. (Most B2B SaaS.)
- Long sentences = literary, premium, slow. (The New Yorker, premium watch brands.)

**Vocabulary**

- Plain (most words 1-2 syllables) = approachable, mass-market.
- Domain-specific = signals expertise to insiders, alienates outsiders.
- Coined or invented = builds a worldview but high risk if not earned (Apple's "Retina", Slack's "huddle").

**Pronouns**

- "We" = the company speaking.
- "You" = the customer being addressed.
- "I" = a person speaking, not a company. Strong on personal brands and creator products. Weird on enterprise.

**Contractions**

- Use them = casual.
- Avoid them = formal.

**Punctuation**

- Em dashes = literary, breath-taking pauses.
- Semicolons = considered, formal, slightly old-school.
- Periods only = punchy, confident.
- Exclamation points = exuberant; lose credibility past one or two.
- Em dashes are popular but polarizing. Many writers and brands ban them outright; some lean on them. Pick a side.

**Rhythm**

Read your copy out loud. If you stumble, the rhythm is off. Vary sentence length. A page of all-short sentences feels staccato. A page of all-long sentences feels lecture-y.

## Worked example: B2B SaaS

**Voice**

> **Direct**, not blunt.
> **Confident**, not cocky.
> **Practical**, not boring.
> **Smart**, not academic.

**Archetype**

Primary: Sage. Secondary: Hero.

**Patterns**

- Sentence length: short to medium. Lead with the headline, deliver the proof, exit.
- Vocabulary: plain, with domain terms when precision earns its keep.
- Pronouns: "we" for the company, "you" for the reader. Avoid "users."
- Contractions: yes.
- Em dashes: no.

**Tone shifts**

- Onboarding: warmer. Lean into "you can do this."
- Error: matter-of-fact. State what happened, what to do, no apology theater.
- Pricing: direct. List the number, list what's included, stop.

**Sample**

Bad (vague, hedged, overwrought):
> Our innovative platform leverages cutting-edge technology to revolutionize how teams collaborate on data-driven workflows.

Good (direct, plain, confident):
> Move data between tools without writing code. Connect your stack in minutes. Built for teams that have outgrown spreadsheets.

## Worked example: D2C food brand

**Voice**

> **Playful**, not goofy.
> **Honest**, not blunt.
> **Crave-worthy**, not gluttonous.
> **Curious**, not academic.

**Archetype**

Primary: Jester. Secondary: Everyman.

**Patterns**

- Sentence length: short. Hit the joke, get out.
- Vocabulary: plain. Sensory words land.
- Pronouns: "we" and "you," very conversational.
- Contractions: always.
- Punctuation: light. Periods do the work.

**Sample**

Bad (corporate, distant):
> We are committed to providing high-quality artisanal granola made with the finest organic ingredients sourced from trusted partners.

Good (playful, sensory, confident):
> Crunchy. Honey-roasted. Made with oats from one farm in Wisconsin. We tried adding more stuff. It got worse. So we stopped.

## When voice is locked

A voice is locked when you can:

1. State it in one sentence ("We sound like a senior engineer explaining something to a junior, with patience.")
2. Cite three brands or writers it borrows from, and what specifically.
3. Show a piece of copy and a critic could say "that does/does not sound like us" without arguing.

If you cannot do all three, keep working on the voice.
