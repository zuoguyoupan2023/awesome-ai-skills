# The Four Axes Explained

Each axis is a spectrum. Pick a position knowing it excludes adjacent positions for this project. Reference brands are illustrative, not prescriptive.

> Worked examples for each axis position can be assembled from any reference brand library that documents tone, aesthetic, audience relationship, and sensory ambition consistently. The framework below describes the four axes; the user's own reference set positions each example along the axes during the brief workflow.

---

## Axis 1: Tone Register

How formal is the language? How much heat does it carry?

### Professional

Measured, precise, low-register. Trusts the reader to do the work of meaning. Restraint is the signal.

**What this signals:** Seriousness, expertise, established status. The brand does not need to perform to be taken seriously.

**Reference examples:** Stripe's documentation. The Economist. McKinsey's published thinking. Bloomberg's editorial style.

**When to pick this:** The audience is making high-stakes decisions and needs to feel they are dealing with peers, not pitchpeople. Or, the category is loud and your differentiation is calm.

**Common failure:** Sliding into stiffness. Professional is not robotic. It is restrained and warm, not cold.

### Conversational

Warmer, more personal, comfortable with first-person and contractions. Reads like a thoughtful person talking, not an institution speaking.

**What this signals:** Approachability without sacrificing competence. A brand that respects the reader as a person, not a target.

**Reference examples:** Linear's product copy. Vercel's marketing voice. Notion's onboarding. MailChimp's voice in their early years.

**When to pick this:** Default for most modern B2B and prosumer brands. The audience appreciates human voice but still needs to trust the brand's competence.

**Common failure:** Sliding into casual. Conversational is still considered. "Hey friend!" is not conversational, it is performed informality.

### Playful

Wit, surprise, willingness to break form for effect. Visible craft in the language itself.

**What this signals:** Confidence, creative ambition, willingness to risk being misread. The brand is having fun and inviting the reader to as well.

**Reference examples:** Slack's early voice. Old Spice campaigns. Bumble. Mailchimp at its peak.

**When to pick this:** The audience expects beige and wants color. The category is mature and differentiation through craft is available. The brand has the cultural permission to be funny without it reading as forced.

**Common failure:** Forcing humor that does not earn its place. Playful only works when it is actually funny. Otherwise it reads as desperate.

### Provocative

Pointed, opinionated, willing to take a position other brands will not. Says what others soften.

**What this signals:** Conviction. The brand stands for something specific and is willing to lose the audience that disagrees in order to bond more deeply with the audience that agrees.

**Reference examples:** Patagonia's political voice. Liquid Death's entire premise. Oatly's category-baiting copy. The early Apple "Think Different" era.

**When to pick this:** Strong opinion exists and hiding it is more expensive than expressing it. The brand can defend the position over the long term, not just at launch.

**Common failure:** Performing provocation without conviction. Audiences detect this in seconds. Either the position is real or the voice fails.

## Axis 2: Aesthetic Philosophy

How much visual density does the work carry? How much does each element earn its place?

### Editorial Restrained

Generous white space, single definitive image instead of grids, considered typography, low color count, deliberate slowness.

**What this signals:** Confidence and patience. The brand is willing to under-fill space because it trusts the reader to fill it with attention.

**Reference examples:** Aesop's website. Apple's product pages in their classic era. Jacquemus. Phaidon's editorial work. Most luxury fashion houses.

**When to pick this:** The audience is fatigued by visual noise. The brand is premium or aspirational. The product or work itself benefits from being viewed slowly.

**Common failure:** Restraint that reads as absence. Restraint earns its place when every remaining element is exquisite. Strip too much and the page reads as unfinished.

### Polished Standard

Modern SaaS aesthetic. Clean grids, balanced contrast, expected proportions, conventional component patterns.

**What this signals:** Competence, professionalism, current. The brand belongs in its category without standing out.

**Reference examples:** Most modern B2B SaaS sites. Stripe's marketing pages. Linear. Notion's marketing. Vercel.

**When to pick this:** The audience expects the category's visual conventions and the brand's differentiation is in the product, not the wrapper. Default for most B2B work.

**Common failure:** Producing work indistinguishable from competitors. Polished Standard is safe. Safe is forgettable.

### Controlled Maximalist

High visual density where every element is intentional. Loud, but engineered. The page is full because each thing earns its place.

**What this signals:** Craft and conviction. The brand has thought about every pixel and chose density on purpose.

**Reference examples:** BUCK's portfolio site. Ueno's work. Pentagram's case study pages. Many top-tier creative agency sites. Some Nike campaign work.

**When to pick this:** The audience expects boring and the brand wants to demonstrate creative ambition. Differentiation through visible craft is available. The team has the design capacity to execute it.

**Common failure:** Density without intent. Controlled Maximalist requires every element to defend its existence. Lazy density reads as cluttered, not crafted.

### Expressive Maximalist

Visual abundance, willingness to be loud, willing to clash. Energy over restraint.

**What this signals:** Energy, ambition, irreverence. The brand is having a moment and the work is the moment.

**Reference examples:** Adult Swim. Cards Against Humanity. Some Heinz creative work. Liquid Death's loudest moments. Music festival branding.

**When to pick this:** The audience is energetic and seeks brands that match. Cultural permission for loudness is high in the category. The brand can sustain the energy over time.

**Common failure:** This is the hardest position to execute well. Expressive Maximalism that lacks craft reads as chaotic, not exciting. Most attempts fail.

## Axis 3: Audience Relationship

How does the brand position itself relative to the reader?

### Authority

We tell you what is true. Expertise on display. Hierarchy implicit and accepted.

**What this signals:** Earned expertise. The reader came here to learn from someone who knows more than they do.

**Reference examples:** Bloomberg. McKinsey's published frameworks. Bain's reports. The Economist. Some of Apple's product copy.

**When to pick this:** The audience explicitly came for expertise. The brand has the credibility to back the position. The category rewards authoritative voice.

**Common failure:** Authority without earned credibility reads as arrogance. The brand needs to actually know more than the reader.

### Peer

We are figuring this out together. Equal footing, shared vocabulary.

**What this signals:** Mutual respect. The reader is treated as a co-thinker, not a learner.

**Reference examples:** Linear. Most developer-focused brands. GitHub. Some of Notion's voice. Most modern B2B SaaS.

**When to pick this:** The audience is sophisticated and reacts badly to being talked down to. The work is genuinely collaborative and acknowledging the reader's expertise serves the relationship.

**Common failure:** False peering. If the brand actually does know more, hiding that to seem peer-like reads as condescending.

### Companion

We walk with you. Lower hierarchy than authority, more presence than peer.

**What this signals:** Sustained accompaniment. The brand is here for the long arc, not just the transaction.

**Reference examples:** Headspace. Calm. Stitch Fix. Some healthcare brands. Anthropic's voice in some moments.

**When to pick this:** The audience is going through something difficult, ongoing, or uncertain. They need presence more than expertise. The relationship is about being with them, not directing them.

**Common failure:** Companion without substance reads as performative warmth. The brand needs to actually do something useful to earn the relationship.

### Coach

We challenge you. The brand pushes the reader toward something they would not push themselves toward alone.

**What this signals:** Productive friction. The brand assumes the reader wants growth and is willing to be pushed.

**Reference examples:** Strava. Whoop. Crossfit's brand voice. Apple Fitness+. Some founder-focused programs.

**When to pick this:** The audience explicitly wants to be challenged. The brand has the credibility to push without being dismissed. The product or service requires effort that the brand is asking of the reader.

**Common failure:** Coaching without permission. The brand needs to be invited into that role. Pushing readers who did not ask to be pushed reads as preachy.

## Axis 4: Sensory Ambition

How much is the work asking of the reader emotionally?

### Functional

Get a job done. Reader gets in, gets the answer, gets out. Aesthetics serve clarity, not feeling.

**What this signals:** Respect for time. The brand assumes the reader has somewhere else to be.

**Reference examples:** Wikipedia. Craigslist. Most documentation sites. Government services that work well. IRS forms when they are well-designed.

**When to pick this:** The reader is in transactional mode. The work's value is utility, not feeling. Asking for emotional engagement would be presumptuous.

**Common failure:** Confusing functional with bare. Functional work is still well-designed. It is just designed for clarity over resonance.

### Considered

Reader notices the craft. Aesthetic choices are visible without being the point.

**What this signals:** Attention to detail without flexing it. The brand cares, and the reader can tell.

**Reference examples:** Apple's product pages. Notion's marketing. Linear's interface. Aesop's product photography. Most premium SaaS.

**When to pick this:** The audience appreciates craft when they encounter it but does not require an emotional payoff. The work needs to feel premium without demanding emotion.

**Common failure:** Considered work that drifts toward decorative. If the craft is visible without earning its place, it reads as fussy.

### Resonant

Reader feels something specific the brand architected. Aesthetics carry meaning. The work is asking the reader to feel.

**What this signals:** Ambition. The brand is trying to do more than communicate. It is trying to move.

**Reference examples:** Hermes. Patagonia at their narrative best. Some Apple campaigns ("Think Different," "Shot on iPhone"). Aesop's editorial work. Most editorial publications.

**When to pick this:** The audience came for feeling, not utility. The brand has the craft to deliver it. The work is significant enough to warrant the emotional ask.

**Common failure:** This is the hardest position. Resonance that is forced reads as manipulation. Resonance that is mistargeted reads as melodrama. Resonance that is vague reads as pretentious. Most attempts fail.

## Combinations to flag

Some combinations are common, some are rare-but-doable, some are contradictory.

**Common combinations:**

- Conversational / Polished Standard / Peer / Considered = the modern B2B SaaS default
- Professional / Editorial Restrained / Authority / Considered = premium consultancy default
- Conversational / Polished Standard / Companion / Considered = wellness or healthcare default

**Rare but powerful:**

- Provocative / Editorial Restrained / Coach / Resonant = the "Patagonia at full conviction" register
- Conversational / Controlled Maximalist / Peer / Resonant = the "top creative agency" register
- Provocative / Controlled Maximalist / Coach / Resonant = the "high-stakes brand revival" register

**Difficult combinations to flag:**

- Functional + Provocative = rare. Provocation usually requires emotional engagement that pure functional work resists.
- Editorial Restrained + Expressive Maximalist = unreachable by selection. These are both positions on Axis 2 (Aesthetic Philosophy), so the user picks one or the other, not both.
- Authority + Functional = possible but flat. Often slides into "we are right and you should know that," which is hard to hold without warming up at least slightly.

When the user picks a difficult combination, surface the difficulty. Ask them to confirm or reconsider. Sometimes the difficulty is the point.
