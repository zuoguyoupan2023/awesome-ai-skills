# Hypothesis Library

Common high-impact CRO hypothesis patterns by funnel stage. Use as starting points; adapt to the specific product and audience. Each pattern includes the typical evidence that supports it and the typical mechanism behind why it works.

---

## Top of funnel: hero and value proposition

### H1: Outcome-led headline beats feature-led

**Hypothesis:** Replacing a feature-led headline ("Advanced workflow automation") with an outcome-led headline ("Ship 30% faster") will increase scroll-past-hero rate and CTA clicks.

**Evidence to look for:**
- Session replays show users bouncing within 3 seconds
- Heatmaps show low engagement below the hero
- The current headline names a feature, not a benefit

**Mechanism:** Users grant 3 to 5 seconds to decide whether to continue. Outcome language is processed faster than feature language.

**Test design:** Hero-only swap. Control vs variant. Primary metric: scroll past hero + CTA click.

---

### H2: Specific number beats vague claim

**Hypothesis:** Replacing "Save time on month-end close" with "Close in 3 days, not 2 weeks" will increase signup rate.

**Mechanism:** Specific numbers are more credible and more memorable than vague claims.

---

### H3: Customer-facing ID over jargon

**Hypothesis:** Replacing internal/industry jargon ("ETL pipeline orchestration") with customer language ("Move data between your tools without engineering") will improve hero engagement.

**Mechanism:** Industry insiders use jargon. Most prospects are not industry insiders.

---

### H4: Real customer photo beats stock

**Hypothesis:** Replacing stock-photo hero imagery with photos of actual customers using the product will increase signup rate.

**Mechanism:** Stock photos are pattern-recognized as marketing fluff. Real customer photos signal authenticity.

---

## Mid-funnel: feature blocks and social proof

### H5: Specific named customer logos beat unnamed

**Hypothesis:** Replacing "Trusted by 1,000+ companies" with named logos will improve trust and conversion.

**Evidence:** Heatmaps show users hovering near social proof but not converting.

**Mechanism:** Named logos provide verifiable trust. Numbers without names are easily fabricated and users know it.

---

### H6: Specific outcome quote beats generic praise

**Hypothesis:** Replacing "We love this product!" with "Reduced our onboarding time by 40 percent in three months" will increase trust score and conversion.

**Mechanism:** Specific outcomes are credible because they're quantifiable. Generic praise is dismissed as cherry-picked.

---

### H7: Founder quote at scale stage

**Hypothesis:** Adding a founder quote and photo on the homepage will increase early-stage trust signals.

**Evidence:** Brand is unknown. No major customer logos to display yet.

**Mechanism:** Personal accountability from a named person beats anonymous brand voice when trust signals are scarce.

---

### H8: Comparison table for competitive categories

**Hypothesis:** Adding a comparison table against named competitors will increase conversion for prospects in active evaluation.

**Mechanism:** Users in evaluation mode are comparing anyway. Honest comparison reduces objection-handling work.

**Risks:** Can backfire if comparison feels biased or if competitors are smaller than you (gives them oxygen).

---

## Form and signup

### H9: Fewer form fields wins

**Hypothesis:** Reducing signup fields from 7 to 3 will increase signup conversion.

**Evidence:** Form analytics show high abandonment, especially on optional fields.

**Mechanism:** Each form field is friction. Optional fields without value to the user feel like data collection for company benefit.

---

### H10: Progressive profiling beats upfront fields

**Hypothesis:** Moving non-essential signup fields to a post-signup onboarding flow will increase initial signup conversion without hurting profile completion.

**Mechanism:** Users commit incrementally. The hardest "yes" is the first one.

---

### H11: Social signup options reduce friction

**Hypothesis:** Adding "Sign up with Google" alongside email signup will increase signup rate.

**Risks:** OAuth providers can also reduce signups for users uncomfortable with social login. Test, don't assume.

---

### H12: Visible password requirements reduce errors

**Hypothesis:** Showing password requirements (8 chars, etc.) above the field instead of as error messages will reduce form errors and abandonment.

**Mechanism:** Errors after submission feel like punishment. Pre-emptive guidance feels like assistance.

---

### H13: Trust signals near form

**Hypothesis:** Adding "Free for 14 days. No credit card." adjacent to the CTA will increase form completion.

**Mechanism:** Reduces perceived risk at the point of commitment.

---

## Pricing page

### H14: Annotated tiers beat unannotated

**Hypothesis:** Adding "Best for" labels on each pricing tier ("Best for solo founders," "Best for teams of 10+") will increase plan selection clarity.

**Mechanism:** Users self-select faster when given decision shortcuts.

---

### H15: Annual emphasis beats monthly default

**Hypothesis:** Defaulting the pricing toggle to annual (with savings highlighted) will increase annual plan adoption.

**Mechanism:** Defaults stick. Loss-framed savings ("Save $X") are more compelling than gain-framed.

---

### H16: Clear "what's included" reduces confusion

**Hypothesis:** Adding a checkmark feature comparison below the pricing tiers will reduce sales-assist requests and improve self-service conversion.

**Mechanism:** Many prospects abandon when they can't quickly verify a needed feature is in their tier.

---

### H17: Social proof on pricing page

**Hypothesis:** Adding tier-specific customer testimonials ("Why we picked Team plan") will increase plan upgrade rate.

**Mechanism:** Tier-specific proof reduces fear of overpaying or under-buying.

---

## Checkout / cart

### H18: Free shipping threshold visible

**Hypothesis:** Adding a "$X away from free shipping" message in the cart will increase average order value.

**Evidence:** Cart abandonment correlated with sub-threshold orders.

**Mechanism:** Goal gradient. Users near a goal accelerate toward it.

---

### H19: Guest checkout option

**Hypothesis:** Adding a guest checkout option will increase conversion of first-time buyers.

**Mechanism:** Account creation is friction at the worst possible moment (when the user has just decided to buy).

---

### H20: Multiple payment methods

**Hypothesis:** Adding [PayPal / Apple Pay / Google Pay / etc.] at checkout will increase mobile conversion.

**Mechanism:** Saved payment methods reduce friction. Mobile typing is painful.

---

### H21: Visible total throughout checkout

**Hypothesis:** Showing the running total persistently in the checkout flow will reduce abandonment.

**Evidence:** Step-by-step abandonment shows users dropping after seeing fees.

**Mechanism:** Surprise cost is the #1 cause of cart abandonment.

---

### H22: Trust badges at checkout

**Hypothesis:** Adding security badges (SSL, PCI, money-back guarantee) at the payment step will increase completion.

**Mechanism:** Security concerns peak at payment entry.

---

## Onboarding (post-signup)

### H23: Single first action vs feature tour

**Hypothesis:** Replacing a feature tour with a single guided first action will increase activation.

**Mechanism:** Feature tours teach. First actions deliver value. Users who experience value retain better than users who learn features.

---

### H24: Sample data on first login

**Hypothesis:** Pre-loading the new user's account with sample data will increase first-week retention.

**Mechanism:** Empty states are demotivating. Pre-populated data shows "what good looks like."

---

### H25: Time-to-value reduction

**Hypothesis:** Reducing the steps from signup to first valuable action from 12 to 4 will increase activation rate.

**Evidence:** Funnel analysis shows huge drops in the middle of the onboarding flow.

**Mechanism:** Each step is a chance to bounce. Fewer steps = more activations.

---

## Email and notifications

### H26: Subject line specificity

**Hypothesis:** Replacing "Update from [Brand]" with a specific benefit-led subject ("Your invoice is ready" or "Save 20% this week") will increase open rate.

**Mechanism:** Inbox is competitive. Specificity earns the open.

---

### H27: Single CTA per email

**Hypothesis:** Reducing email CTAs from 3 to 1 will increase click-through to primary action.

**Mechanism:** Choice paralysis. Multiple CTAs dilute the primary action.

---

### H28: Personalization beyond name

**Hypothesis:** Personalizing email body content based on user behavior (last action, product viewed) will increase reactivation rate.

**Mechanism:** Generic emails feel like spam. Behaviorally relevant emails feel like service.

---

## Mobile-specific

### H29: Tap target sizing

**Hypothesis:** Increasing tap targets to meet 44x44 minimum will reduce mobile bounce rate.

**Evidence:** Mobile heatmaps show mis-taps and rage clicks.

**Mechanism:** Small targets fail more often than large ones.

---

### H30: Sticky CTA on mobile

**Hypothesis:** Adding a sticky bottom CTA on mobile pages will increase conversion.

**Mechanism:** Long mobile pages bury CTAs. Sticky keeps the action available.

**Risks:** Can cover content. Test for accessibility issues.

---

### H31: Mobile-specific headlines

**Hypothesis:** Shortening headlines for mobile viewports (vs desktop) will reduce bounce rate.

**Mechanism:** Long headlines on mobile become walls of text. Short headlines preserve hierarchy.

---

## Compounding tests

The biggest CRO wins come from compounding learnings. After running 5 to 10 tests, you should know:

- What kind of headlines win in your category
- How long your forms should be
- Which trust signals move your audience
- Which features matter to your buyer
- What price framing converts

These insights compound across tests. Document each test's lesson, even when the test loses.

A pattern to watch:
- **Test #1:** Outcome-led headline wins
- **Test #5:** Outcome-led email subject wins
- **Test #10:** Outcome-led ad copy wins

You haven't just run 10 tests. You've discovered that "outcome-led messaging" is your audience's preference, broadly. That insight is worth more than any single test.
