# Subject Line Patterns

Subject lines are the deciding factor for opens. A great email with a weak subject gets archived unread.

These patterns are tools, not formulas. Apply them, then make them sound like the brand.

---

## Universal rules

- **Length:** 30 to 50 characters. Mobile clients truncate longer.
- **No ALL CAPS.** Reads as shouting and triggers spam filters.
- **No excessive punctuation.** "!!!" reads as desperate.
- **Match the body.** A subject that doesn't deliver in the body kills trust.
- **Sentence case** is more readable than Title Case in inboxes.
- **Skip the prefix conventions** ("Newsletter #23"). Subscribers ignore them.

---

## Pattern 1: Specific outcome

Names what the reader gets.

**Examples:**
- "The 5 questions to ask before hiring a designer"
- "Your team's productivity numbers for September"
- "How to cut your invoicing time in half"
- "What changed in your competitor's pricing this month"

**Strengths:** Clear value. Easy to evaluate whether to open.

**When to use:** Newsletters, content emails, value-driven sends.

---

## Pattern 2: Curiosity gap

Opens a loop the email body closes.

**Examples:**
- "The mistake most teams make in week 2"
- "Why your best customers are leaving"
- "The one habit your top performers share"
- "What I wish I'd known before scaling to 50 people"

**Strengths:** High open rates when the gap is genuine.

**Risks:** Becomes click-bait if the body doesn't deliver. Erodes trust over time.

**When to use:** Sparingly. The body must deliver real insight.

---

## Pattern 3: Personal / direct

Speaks as if to a friend.

**Examples:**
- "Quick question, [name]"
- "Saw this and thought of you"
- "Got 5 minutes?"
- "Are you still using [tool]?"

**Strengths:** Feels human. Cuts through marketing-speak.

**Risks:** Used by every spam email. Authentic context required for it to work.

**When to use:** 1:1 sends, personalized cold outreach, founder-to-customer emails.

---

## Pattern 4: Direct / no-mystery

States exactly what the email is about.

**Examples:**
- "Your receipt for order #12345"
- "Password reset link"
- "Invoice for September"
- "Meeting confirmation: Tuesday 2pm"

**Strengths:** High trust. High open rates for transactional content.

**When to use:** Transactional emails. Always be direct.

---

## Pattern 5: Question

Poses the question the reader is asking.

**Examples:**
- "Should you hire a generalist or specialist first?"
- "Is your pricing too cheap?"
- "What's the ROI of customer service?"
- "Are you measuring the wrong KPIs?"

**Strengths:** Aligns with the reader's mental state. Strong for educational content.

**When to use:** Newsletters and educational sequences.

---

## Pattern 6: Number / list

Promises a specific quantity of items.

**Examples:**
- "3 things to do this week to retain customers"
- "7 signs your team is about to burn out"
- "The 4-step checklist for a clean handoff"

**Strengths:** Sets clear expectations. Easy to scan.

**Risks:** Overused. Can read as listicle filler.

**When to use:** When the content genuinely is a list and the count matters.

---

## Pattern 7: Time-bound / urgency

Signals the email is time-sensitive.

**Examples:**
- "Last day: free shipping on your order"
- "Webinar starts in 2 hours"
- "Pricing changes Thursday"

**Strengths:** Drives immediate action.

**Risks:** Overuse trains subscribers to ignore urgency cues. Reserve for genuine deadlines.

**When to use:** Real time-sensitive sends only. Never fake urgency.

---

## Pattern 8: News / update

States what's new.

**Examples:**
- "New: instant export to spreadsheets"
- "Just launched: team mode"
- "Update on the integration you asked for"

**Strengths:** Useful for product update emails.

**When to use:** Real product announcements that the recipient signed up to hear about.

---

## Pattern 9: Personal anecdote / story

Hints at a story.

**Examples:**
- "I almost didn't ship this"
- "What happened when we tried [thing]"
- "The phone call that changed our hiring process"

**Strengths:** Strong for narrative-driven brand newsletters.

**When to use:** When the brand voice supports storytelling.

---

## Pattern 10: Reference to recipient's behavior

Acknowledges what they did.

**Examples:**
- "Saw you tried [feature] - here's what's next"
- "Welcome back. Here's what you missed."
- "You completed week 1. Here's week 2."

**Strengths:** Personalization that actually feels personal.

**When to use:** Behavior-triggered emails. Lifecycle and onboarding sequences.

---

## Subject lines by sequence type

### Welcome / onboarding

```
Email 1: Welcome to [Brand], [name]
Email 2: The first thing to do in [Brand]
Email 3: How [audience] use [Brand] (3 examples)
Email 4: Did this happen for you?
Email 5: 30 days in: where you could be
```

### Lifecycle / activation

```
Trigger: completed milestone
"Nice work hitting [milestone]. Here's what's next."

Trigger: stalled on a step
"Quick question about your setup"
```

### Re-engagement / win-back

```
Email 1: Hey [name], we miss you
Email 2: Here's [specific incentive]
Email 3: Last email - should we say goodbye?
```

### Newsletter

```
Curiosity: "The metric most teams ignore"
Specific: "September wrap: what we shipped, what we learned"
Question: "Should you raise your prices?"
Number: "3 reads from this week"
```

### Transactional

```
"Your order is on the way (#12345)"
"Receipt for your subscription"
"Your password was changed"
"Action required: confirm your email"
```

### Broadcast

```
Launch: "Just shipped: [feature]"
Event: "Live this Thursday: [topic]"
Update: "Our pricing changed today. Here's what it means for you."
```

## A/B test ideas

Subject lines are the easiest thing to test. For any meaningful send, generate 2 to 4 variations along these axes:

- **Specific vs. curiosity:** Does the audience prefer to know what they're getting, or to be intrigued?
- **Personal vs. direct:** Does first-person ("I just shipped this") work better than third-person ("Just launched")?
- **Length:** Short (under 30 chars) vs. medium (30 to 50 chars).
- **Punctuation:** Question vs. statement.
- **Personalization:** With name vs. without.

Track open rates and click-through rates. Open alone is incomplete; some subject patterns get opened but discarded.

## What to avoid

### "Just checking in"
Empty. Adds no value. Reads as nag.

### "Following up"
Same problem. The reader knows you're following up. Tell them why.

### "Thoughts?"
A request without context. Comes across as lazy.

### "Hi"
Just don't. Even from someone you know.

### Excessive emojis
🎉🚀✨ does not signal value. Often signals spam to filters.

### Re: when it's not actually a reply
Deceptive. Damages trust.

### Personalization gone wrong
"{first_name} wanted to chat" with the variable un-rendered. Test send before broadcast.

## Preview text (preheader)

The line that appears next to or below the subject. Often more important than the subject for engagement.

**Best practice:**
- Use it as the subject's continuation
- 50 to 90 characters
- Don't waste it on "View in browser" or boilerplate
- Add the detail the subject didn't fit

**Examples of subject + preview pairs:**

| Subject | Preview |
|---|---|
| "Your week 1 progress" | "3 of 5 steps complete. Here's what's next." |
| "The mistake most teams make" | "(It's not the obvious one)" |
| "Just shipped: team mode" | "Invite up to 50 teammates and assign roles." |

A weak preview wastes prime real estate. A strong preview can save a mediocre subject.
