# Category conventions

Every category has a default visual register the audience expects. Working inside the default signals competence; the brand reads as belonging. Working outside the default signals positioning; the brand reads as making a deliberate move. Both are legitimate. The trap is doing either by accident.

This file surveys ten major brand categories at parity. For each: the defaults that work, the conventions worth honoring, the conventions worth breaking for positioning, the cliches to avoid, the application contexts unique to the category, and reference brands as visual shorthand.

---

## 1. Legal firms

The category is dominated by heritage positioning. White-shoe firms, century-old partnerships, names tied to founding partners' surnames. The audience reading the logo is general counsel, founders raising or selling, CFOs in industries where the wrong attorney is materially expensive. The buyer brings high stakes and trusts visual signals of institutional gravity.

### Defaults that work

- **Old-style or transitional serif typography** for the wordmark. Garamond, Caslon, Sabon, Source Serif. The serif reads as institutional and serious.
- **Lockup with monogram** OR **wordmark with monogram fallback**. The full firm name is often long (multiple partner surnames) so the monogram does heavy lifting in constrained contexts.
- **Restrained color palette.** Navy, deep charcoal, oxblood, deep forest green. No primary red, no bright blue, no anything that reads as casual.
- **Single-color reproduction** built into the system from day one (court filings reproduce in black on white, foil stamps on letterhead are common, embossed signage on lobby walls).

### Conventions worth honoring

- Heritage signaling through old-style serif type and monogram architecture
- Single-color reproducibility for court filings and formal documents
- Geometric framing (ring, shield) only in restrained executions; heavy ornament reads as fake heritage

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Geometric sans for the wordmark signals "modern, accessible" (boutique firms, tech-adjacent practices, plaintiff-side firms)
- Lighter color palette signals approachability (firms competing for client comfort, not gravity)
- Asymmetric lockup signals contemporary execution (firms positioning against the traditional white-shoe model)

### Common cliches to avoid

- The Roman-pillar-as-justice. Pillars are exhausted.
- The scales-of-justice icon. Done thousands of times; signals nothing.
- The gavel. Cliche.
- The lady-Justice silhouette. Same.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Court filings (single-color black on white at small scale)
- Embossed letterhead and business cards (the foil-stamp test is real)
- Lobby and conference room signage (engraved brass, etched glass)
- Email signatures with the firm wordmark and individual attorney name
- Billable-hours invoices (the wordmark must look reproducible at low quality)

### Reference brands

- **Latham & Watkins** (LW monogram in serif, conservative palette)
- **Wachtell, Lipton, Rosen & Katz** (full firm name as wordmark, no symbol; positioning as "we don't need a symbol")
- **Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom** (Skadden monogram, restrained sans)

## 2. Consumer goods / CPG

The category is dominated by retail competition. The mark must register at shelf distance (10 to 15 feet), at thumbnail scale (online product imagery), and at rapid scan speed (the shopper has 2 seconds per shelf section). Distinctiveness is survival.

### Defaults that work

- **Custom display typography** for the wordmark. The brand owns its letterforms because the wordmark is on every package.
- **Lockup with literal symbol** OR **strong wordmark only**. The symbol depicts what the brand is about; the wordmark carries the brand name.
- **Bold color palettes.** Bright colors register at distance. Restraint is the exception.
- **Pattern systems** that extend the brand identity onto packaging surfaces.

### Conventions worth honoring

- Distinctiveness at small scale (online product thumbnails, 80x80 pixels)
- Single-color or simplified versions for embossed packaging, foil stamps, hot-metal printing
- Color recognition: the brand color is the first signal on shelf, before the wordmark even reads

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Restrained typography signals premium positioning (Method, Brandless, Aesop)
- Black-and-white-only signals minimalist or anti-CPG positioning
- Hand-drawn or organic letterforms signal craft or small-batch positioning

### Common cliches to avoid

- The swoosh-as-growth. Borrowed from Nike.
- The leaf-as-natural. Done thousands of times in wellness and organic.
- The dot-or-circle-as-modernity. The 2010s startup tier.
- The hand-drawn brushstroke under the wordmark. Saturated.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Retail packaging at shelf scale
- Online product thumbnails (80x80 to 200x200 pixels)
- Print catalog imagery (offset print, sometimes single-color)
- In-store signage and end-cap displays
- Branded merchandise (hats, totes, t-shirts the brand sells)

### Reference brands

- **Method** (custom geometric sans, restrained palette, premium positioning in a category dominated by aggressive color)
- **Hu Chocolate** (hand-drawn wordmark, organic typography, craft positioning)
- **Brandless** (anti-brand brand: minimalist sans, monochrome, the wordmark IS the absence of conventional branding)

## 3. B2B SaaS

The category is dominated by visual interchangeability. Most B2B SaaS brands use neo-grotesque sans typography, geometric reduction symbols, and a primary color in the blue-to-purple range. The mark blurs into the category default unless the brand makes deliberate distinguishing choices.

### Defaults that work

- **Neo-grotesque sans typography** (Inter, Söhne, GT America, custom). The default register reads as competent and contemporary.
- **Lockup with abstract or geometric-reduction symbol**. The symbol carries small-size and app-icon contexts.
- **Single-accent color palette.** A primary brand color plus monochrome, no busy palette.
- **Custom letterform** in the wordmark to escape the interchangeable default.

### Conventions worth honoring

- Screen rendering optimization (the wordmark must read at every screen size and DPI)
- App icon construction (the symbol must work inside rounded-square and circle masks)
- Monochrome reproducibility (single-color and reverse-on-dark should both work)

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Old-style serif signals "we take ourselves seriously" (rare in SaaS; positions against the modern startup tier)
- Bright primary color (red, orange, yellow) signals energy in a category that defaults to blue and gray
- Custom display typography signals long-term brand investment in a category where most brands look interchangeable

### Common cliches to avoid

- The geometric hexagon. The most overused symbol in tech.
- The three-bars stacked horizontally. The 2018-2020 SaaS tier.
- The triangle pointing right. Done.
- The bisected circle. Same.
- The single-letter-inside-circle without distinctive letterform construction.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- App icons (iOS, Android, web app, browser extension)
- Marketing site at desktop scale
- Documentation site at small scale (favicon, header, footer)
- In-product brand display (loading screens, empty states, account avatars)
- Slack and email integration logos (small thumbnails)

### Reference brands

- **Stripe** (custom Sohne wordmark, abstract gesture symbol, restrained palette)
- **Linear** (Inter wordmark, geometric reduction symbol, signature single-color execution)
- **Vercel** (custom Geist wordmark, geometric reduction triangle, bold black-and-white)
- **Notion** (custom letterform with notebook reference)

## 4. Hospitality (hotels, restaurants, resorts)

The category sits between heritage luxury and contemporary craft. The audience experiences the brand across many surfaces simultaneously: building exteriors, interior signage, room key cards, menus, uniforms, towels, sheets, packaging. Multi-surface coherence is the discipline.

### Defaults that work

- **Custom display typography** OR **transitional serif** for the wordmark. The brand often becomes the visual identity of the property.
- **Lockup with monogram fallback**. The monogram appears on towels, sheets, room keys, embroidered uniforms.
- **Restrained palette** with a single signature color. The palette extends to interior design, materials, lighting.
- **Embroidery-grade variant** built into the system from day one.

### Conventions worth honoring

- Embroidery on uniforms, towels, robes, sheets (the monogram does this work)
- Foil-stamp on stationery, room key cards, room service menus
- Engraved or etched signage on building exteriors and interior wayfinding
- Restraint in palette (heritage hospitality avoids loud color)

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Modern geometric sans signals "boutique, design-forward" (Ace Hotel, Standard Hotels)
- Bold color signals contemporary or playful positioning
- Asymmetric lockup signals contemporary execution
- Display custom typography signals investment in a unique brand identity

### Common cliches to avoid

- The crown-as-luxury. Saturated in heritage hospitality.
- The shield-with-year-founded. Every craft beer label uses this.
- The crossed-keys (Hermes-derived). Done.
- The mountain-or-palm-tree literal symbol for resort properties. Done.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Building exterior signage (channel letters, illuminated)
- Room key cards (foil stamp on plastic or paper)
- Towels, robes, sheets (embroidery)
- Glassware, china, silverware (etched, engraved)
- Stationery (letterhead, envelopes, business cards, foil stamp)
- Uniforms (embroidered name tags and lapel pins)
- Bag tags and luggage labels

### Reference brands

- **Aman Resorts** (restrained typographic identity, Asian-influenced design discipline)
- **Soho House** (custom display typography, monogram primary, modern member-club positioning)
- **Ace Hotel** (custom display typography, contemporary boutique positioning, regional variation across properties)

## 5. Healthcare

The category is dominated by trust signaling. The audience may be anxious, stressed, or grieving. The brand must read as competent and calm without reading as cold or clinical. Trust is built through restraint, not through aggressive visual claims.

### Defaults that work

- **Humanist sans typography** for the wordmark. The calligraphic warmth carries trust.
- **Lockup with abstract gesture symbol**. The symbol suggests care, balance, or healing without depicting it literally.
- **Calm color palette.** Blue, green, soft warm neutrals dominate.
- **Plenty of whitespace** in the brand system. Density reads as anxiety.

### Conventions worth honoring

- Accessibility floor: WCAG AA contrast ratios on every brand color combination
- Restrained palette: no aggressive red (reads as emergency or alarm)
- Avoid imagery that could be misread (a symbol that suggests a religious cross when the brand is secular, a symbol that suggests a specific medical specialty when the brand is generalist)

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Modern geometric sans signals "tech-forward primary care" (One Medical, Forward)
- Warmer color palette (coral, peach, soft yellow) signals "we are not your old doctor's office"
- Custom display typography signals contemporary positioning (rare in legacy healthcare)

### Common cliches to avoid

- The medical cross. Trademark-protected by the Red Cross in some uses; otherwise generic.
- The caduceus (snakes around a staff). Misread by audiences as Hermes; not actually the medical symbol.
- The heartbeat-line. Saturated in fitness and healthcare.
- The blue-and-green gradient. The default healthcare palette to a fault.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Wayfinding signage in clinics and hospitals
- Patient-facing materials (intake forms, appointment reminders, billing statements)
- Medical equipment branding (the wordmark on a screen, on a printed report)
- Insurance and billing communications (the wordmark on bills the patient hopes are accurate)
- Clinic uniforms and badges

### Reference brands

- **One Medical** (humanist sans wordmark, simplified circle symbol, restrained palette, primary care positioning)
- **Forward** (custom display typography, geometric reduction symbol, tech-forward primary care)
- **Hims** (custom geometric sans, single-color execution, modern men's health positioning)

## 6. Financial services

The category splits between heritage and fintech-modern. Heritage banks (Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley) lean into old-style serif and institutional gravity. Fintech challengers (Ramp, Mercury, Stripe) lean into neo-grotesque sans and modern restraint. The split is itself a positioning decision.

### Defaults that work

- **Old-style serif** OR **neo-grotesque sans** (the heritage-vs-fintech axis)
- **Lockup with monogram fallback** (heritage) OR **wordmark with abstract symbol** (fintech)
- **Restrained palette.** Navy, gold, deep green for heritage. Black, white, single-accent for fintech.
- **Single-color reproducibility** built into the system (foil stamps, embossed checks, single-color regulatory documents)

### Conventions worth honoring

- Single-color reproducibility (foil stamps, regulatory filings, embossed checks)
- Trust signals through restraint and material quality (heavy paper, foil stamps)
- Color choice signals heritage (navy, gold, deep green) vs fintech (black, white, electric accent)

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Modern geometric sans in heritage financial services signals "boutique, contemporary" (rare and high-stakes)
- Bright color in heritage financial services signals "we are not your father's bank" (high-risk positioning)
- Old-style serif in fintech signals "we take regulatory compliance seriously" (positions against fast-and-loose fintech)

### Common cliches to avoid

- The eagle-as-strength. Heritage cliche.
- The shield-as-security. Cliche across heritage and fintech.
- The bull-as-bullish. Wall Street cliche.
- The geometric chevron pointing up. Fintech cliche.
- The blue-and-gold gradient. Heritage default that reads as visually unconsidered.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Embossed credit and debit cards (the mark is impressed, not printed)
- Regulatory filings and disclosures (single-color reproduction)
- Bank checks (single-color print, MICR-encoded routing numbers)
- Branch signage (channel letters, illuminated)
- ATM screens and branded interfaces

### Reference brands

- **Goldman Sachs** (old-style serif wordmark, restrained palette, heritage positioning)
- **Ramp** (custom Söhne wordmark, abstract symbol, modern restrained palette)
- **Mercury** (custom geometric sans, abstract gesture symbol, fintech positioning with restraint)

## 7. Editorial / publishing

The category is dominated by typography. The masthead IS the logo. Most editorial brands have no separate symbol; the wordmark in a specific custom serif IS the entire identity. The discipline is letterform and proportional refinement.

### Defaults that work

- **Custom display typography** OR **old-style serif** OR **transitional serif** for the wordmark
- **Wordmark only** OR **wordmark with monogram fallback**
- **Black-on-white** as the default reproduction (newsstand, print, digital header)
- **Restrained palette** (typically a single accent color, often the brand's signature)

### Conventions worth honoring

- Single-color reproducibility (every newspaper masthead must work in black on white at masthead scale)
- Custom letterform investment (the masthead is the brand's most-rendered asset)
- Typographic heritage (the masthead often references the publication's founding-era typography)

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Geometric sans signals contemporary editorial (Vox, BuzzFeed, Vice attempted versions of this)
- Bold color signals contemporary positioning (rare in legacy editorial)
- Modern asymmetric layouts signal contemporary craft

### Common cliches to avoid

- The blackletter masthead. Saturated for newspapers; reads as German tabloid.
- The decorative initial cap drop. Editorial cliche.
- The pen-nib symbol for writing or journalism brands. Cliche.
- The book-and-ribbon. Cliche.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Newsstand at street scale (the masthead must read at 50 to 100 feet)
- Print body and headline typography (the wordmark sits at the top of every printed page)
- Digital masthead at desktop and mobile scale
- Subscription and renewal direct mail (single-color print)
- Cover photography (the masthead overlays photographic backgrounds)

### Reference brands

- **The Atlantic** (custom transitional serif, restrained palette, editorial heritage)
- **The New Yorker** (custom Caslon-derived masthead, the mark is unmistakable)
- **Cereal** (custom display sans, considered minimalism, contemporary editorial)

## 8. Tech / AI

The category is dominated by abstract gesture and geometric reduction. Most AI and tech brands lean into one of two modes: either neo-grotesque sans with abstract gesture symbol (Anthropic's flower, OpenAI's geometric mark) or aggressive geometric reduction (every blockchain logo, every web3 brand). Visual fatigue in the category is real.

### Defaults that work

- **Neo-grotesque sans** OR **custom geometric sans** for the wordmark
- **Lockup with abstract gesture or geometric reduction symbol**
- **Single-accent color palette.** Often the brand's "signature color" plus monochrome.
- **Construction grid discipline** in the symbol

### Conventions worth honoring

- Screen rendering optimization (the brand lives on screens at every size)
- App icon construction (rounded-square and circle masks, every iOS/Android size)
- Monochrome and reverse-on-dark variants

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Old-style serif signals "we take ourselves seriously" (rare in tech, signals against the dominant aesthetic)
- Hand-drawn or organic letterforms signal craft or human-centered positioning
- Bright primary color signals energy in a sea of muted palettes

### Common cliches to avoid

- The geometric hexagon-as-blockchain. The most overused symbol in crypto.
- The neural-network-circles symbol for AI brands. Done thousands of times.
- The brain-with-circuit-traces. Cliche.
- The triangle-with-an-eye. Cliche.
- The default purple gradient. The 2018-2022 tech tier.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Developer documentation sites (the wordmark sits in dense technical contexts)
- API and CLI integrations (the brand appears in terminal output, error messages, log lines)
- Loading screens and empty states in product
- Open-source repository profile pictures
- Conference talk slide branding

### Reference brands

- **Anthropic** (custom display typography, abstract gesture symbol, restrained palette)
- **OpenAI** (custom geometric mark, simple wordmark, monochrome execution)
- **Hugging Face** (custom emoji-as-symbol, distinctive in a category that doesn't usually allow it)

## 9. Outdoor / lifestyle / apparel

The category sits at the intersection of illustration, heritage, and embroidery. Many outdoor brands use illustration-driven marks with hand-drawn or vintage-inspired execution. The application context is frequently embroidered (apparel, hats, packs) so the mark must survive thread on fabric.

### Defaults that work

- **Custom display typography** OR **slab serif** for the wordmark
- **Lockup with literal symbol** depicting the brand's outdoor world (mountain, river, tree, animal silhouette)
- **Heritage color palette.** Earth tones, warm reds, deep greens, cream-and-coffee
- **Embroidery-grade variant** built into the primary system

### Conventions worth honoring

- Embroidery on apparel, hats, and packs (the mark must survive 4-6 colors on thread)
- Patch-friendly construction for hats and uniforms
- Hand-drawn or vintage-inspired execution that signals craft and authenticity
- Color palette referencing the brand's outdoor environment

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Modern geometric sans signals contemporary outdoor (Topo Designs, Outdoor Voices)
- Bright color signals younger or athletic positioning
- Wordmark-only signals premium or design-led positioning (rare)

### Common cliches to avoid

- The bear silhouette. Saturated for outdoor and lifestyle.
- The mountain peak with a sun rising. Done.
- The compass rose. Saturated for travel and outdoor.
- The Pacific-Northwest cabin aesthetic. Saturated since 2015.
- The tent silhouette. Cliche.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Apparel embroidery (hats, polos, jackets, packs)
- Patch embroidery (jackets, packs, vests)
- Heat-transfer printing on technical fabrics
- Hangtags and care labels on apparel
- Retail signage at small specialty stores

### Reference brands

- **Patagonia** (custom display typography, Fitz Roy mountain silhouette, signature gradient palette)
- **REI** (custom slab serif wordmark, the wedge symbol, outdoor heritage positioning)
- **Filson** (custom slab serif, monogram and shield treatments, heritage workwear positioning)
- **Topo Designs** (custom display typography, contemporary outdoor positioning, designed in Colorado)

## 10. Fashion / luxury

The category is dominated by monogram and ligature conventions. Most heritage luxury brands use monograms (Chanel CN, Gucci GG, Yves Saint Laurent YSL, Louis Vuitton LV). Modern accessible-luxury brands lean toward custom display typography (The Row, Aimé Leon Dore, Jacquemus). The split is the positioning question.

### Defaults that work

- **Old-style serif** OR **custom display typography** OR **ligature monogram** for the primary mark
- **Lockup with monogram fallback** OR **wordmark with monogram on goods**
- **Restrained palette.** Black, white, gold, and the brand's signature accent
- **Embroidery-grade variant** for apparel and accessories

### Conventions worth honoring

- Embroidery on apparel and accessories (the monogram does this work)
- Foil stamp and emboss on packaging (the wordmark or monogram impressed in metallic foil)
- Material quality (heavy paper, leather, metallic foil)
- Restraint in palette and ornamentation

### Conventions worth breaking for positioning

- Modern geometric sans signals accessible-luxury (The Row, Aimé Leon Dore lean restrained-modern)
- Bright color signals contemporary or playful luxury (rare and risky)
- Hand-drawn or expressive letterforms signal craft positioning

### Common cliches to avoid

- The crown-as-luxury. Saturated in fashion and hospitality.
- The decorative monogram with filigree. Reads as Renaissance Faire, not as Hermès.
- The shield-with-year-founded. Done.
- The gold-foil-with-Old-English-typeface. Cliche fake-heritage.

### Application contexts unique to the category

- Apparel embroidery (logos, monograms, signatures stitched into garments)
- Hardware embossing (buttons, rivets, zipper pulls, leather goods)
- Foil stamp on packaging (boxes, dust bags, ribbon)
- Engraved or etched hardware (jewelry, watches)
- Vellum and tissue paper printing (gift wrap, bag liners)
- Window displays at flagship stores

### Reference brands

- **Chanel** (CN monogram, no symbol beyond initials, the most institutionally consistent fashion brand)
- **Gucci** (GG monogram, custom display typography for the wordmark)
- **The Row** (custom display typography, restrained accessible-luxury positioning)
- **Aimé Leon Dore** (custom display typography, contemporary heritage positioning, restrained monogram on apparel)

## How to use this file

When designing for a category not your own, start with the defaults. They exist because they work. The audience reads them as competence.

When designing for a category you want to position against, identify which convention you are breaking and why. The break should be deliberate and supported by the rest of the brand system. A boutique law firm in geometric sans signals modern positioning IF the rest of the brand (color, photography, voice, materials) also signals modern positioning. Breaking type without breaking the rest of the system reads as random.

When designing without category context, the defaults are still the right starting point. Most brands sit inside a category. Most brands benefit from being recognizable inside that category before they push for distinctiveness. Distinctiveness without belonging reads as outsider; belonging without distinctiveness reads as forgettable. The discipline is to start with the category default and earn each departure.
