# Typographic registers

Type carries the wordmark. Type carries half the lockup. Type drives more of a logo's read than any other single decision because type is the voice of the brand, frozen. This file expands the seven registers listed in [SKILL.md](../SKILL.md) with named typefaces, category-fit notes, example brands, pairing guidance, and the failure mode each register tends toward.

---

## Geometric sans

Built from circles and verticals. Letterforms are constructed from primitive geometric shapes (circle, square, triangle) with consistent stroke weight and minimal optical adjustment. Futura, Avenir, Avenir Next, Cabinet Grotesk, ITC Avant Garde, Gotham, Proxima Nova.

Geometric sans reads modern, considered, often optimistic. The discipline of the geometry signals "we made deliberate choices". Used well, it carries restraint. Used poorly, it reads as cold or interchangeable with every other modern startup.

Futura was the original (1927, Paul Renner). Avenir is Adrian Frutiger's warmer, more humanist take on the same idea (1988). Avenir Next is the modern release with broader weights and language coverage. Cabinet Grotesk is a recent geometric sans that became a signature of the 2020-2023 startup wave (and now reads dated for that reason). Gotham (Tobias Frere-Jones) is the American-vernacular geometric sans, derived from New York signage; warmer than Futura, more grounded than Avenir.

### Categories that default to geometric sans

- Tech startups (the modern default; risk of looking like every other startup)
- Architecture and design firms (where the geometry signals craft)
- Modern hospitality (boutique hotels, restaurant groups built post-2010)
- Wellness and fitness brands at the considered end (not the energetic end)
- Real estate at the modern positioning end (urban, design-forward)

### Example brands

- Spotify (custom geometric sans, derived from Circular)
- Airbnb (Cereal, custom geometric sans by Dalton Maag)
- Headspace (Apercu Pro, geometric humanist sans)
- Nike's wordmark (custom Futura-derived)

### Pairing notes

Geometric sans pairs well with old-style serifs (the contrast between modern geometry and classical curves reads as intentional) and with slab serifs of similar weight class (both are structurally heavy, the contrast is in the silhouette). Avoid pairing geometric sans with neo-grotesque sans; the two registers are too close in feel and the pairing reads as indecisive.

### Common pitfalls

- The trend-chasing risk. Cabinet Grotesk became "2022-2023 startup typeface"; using it now codes the brand as late to a trend.
- The cold-tech risk. Pure geometric sans can feel emotionally distant. Warming requires color, custom letterform, or strong context.
- The interchangeability risk. Geometric sans wordmarks all start to look the same after a category saturates. Distinctiveness comes from custom letterforms or weight/proportion choices, not just typeface selection.

## Humanist sans

Has serif-like calligraphic gestures without serifs. The strokes vary in weight (subtle thick-thin contrast). The letterforms have movement, not just geometry. Gill Sans, Optima, Frutiger, Source Sans Pro, Inter at higher optical sizes, Hermann Zapf's Kabel, Jeremy Tankard's Bliss.

Humanist sans reads professional but warm. The calligraphic origin gives the type a hand, where geometric sans has none. Excellent neutral choice when the category demands restraint with personality. Old enough to feel timeless; not old enough to feel dated.

Gill Sans (Eric Gill, 1928) is the original British humanist sans, derived from Edward Johnston's London Underground typeface. Optima (Hermann Zapf, 1958) is the most calligraphic of the humanist sans, with strong thick-thin contrast that reads as sophisticated. Frutiger was designed for Charles de Gaulle airport signage in 1976 and has been the workhorse humanist sans for transit and wayfinding ever since. Source Sans Pro is Adobe's open-source humanist sans (Paul Hunt, 2012); Inter (Rasmus Andersson) crosses humanist and neo-grotesque registers depending on optical size.

### Categories that default to humanist sans

- Professional services (consulting, agencies, professional partnerships)
- Education and academic publishing
- Healthcare at the calm end (One Medical, Forward)
- Civic and institutional work (museums, libraries, public services)
- Editorial brands at the modern restrained end

### Example brands

- BBC (Gill Sans)
- BMW Group's corporate identity (custom humanist sans, derived from Helvetica with humanist warming)
- One Medical (FF Tisa Sans, humanist with slab adjacency)
- Various university identities (Penn, Stanford, MIT all use humanist or near-humanist marks)

### Pairing notes

Humanist sans pairs well with old-style serifs (the calligraphic origins of both registers complement each other) and with transitional serifs (humanist sans for body, transitional serif for display creates a coherent editorial system). Avoid pairing humanist sans with geometric sans; the calligraphic warmth of humanist clashes with the geometric coldness.

### Common pitfalls

- The "British civic" risk. Gill Sans is so coded as British civic that using it for a non-British brand can feel anachronistic.
- The dated risk for old humanist sans. Frutiger reads as 1990s transit unless deployed deliberately. Optima reads as 1970s pharmaceutical unless restored to its original sophistication.
- The lukewarm risk. Humanist sans is the safest choice; it can also be the most forgettable. Distinctiveness still requires custom letterform or strong color discipline.

## Neo-grotesque sans

The modern professional default. Helvetica, Inter, Aktiv Grotesk, Söhne, GT America, Univers, Akzidenz-Grotesk. Neo-grotesque is descended from 19th-century grotesque sans typefaces but rationalized for the modern era. Even stroke weight, closed apertures, neutral letterform construction.

Neo-grotesque reads competent and contemporary. It is the typographic equivalent of a black t-shirt: tasteful, defensible, easy to specify. The trade-off is that everyone else is wearing the same black t-shirt. Without strong color, distinctive symbol, or considered execution, a neo-grotesque wordmark blends into the category default.

Helvetica (Max Miedinger, 1957) is the prototype. Akzidenz-Grotesk preceded it (1898) but Helvetica became the dominant version. Söhne (Klim Type Foundry, 2019) is the modern revival of the neo-grotesque tradition, with sharper detailing and broader weights. GT America (Grilli Type) crosses American grotesque and Swiss neo-grotesque traditions. Inter (Rasmus Andersson, 2016) is the modern open-source workhorse, designed for screen rendering at every size.

### Categories that default to neo-grotesque sans

- B2B SaaS (the unmistakable default; nearly every modern SaaS brand uses Inter, Söhne, or a custom neo-grotesque)
- Modern financial services (Stripe, Mercury, Ramp all use neo-grotesque or near-neo-grotesque)
- Tech at scale (Apple's San Francisco is a neo-grotesque-influenced humanist; Google's Product Sans is neo-grotesque-influenced geometric)
- Modern professional services rejecting the humanist sans default
- Editorial brands at the contemporary end (Bloomberg, modern news brands)

### Example brands

- Stripe (custom Sohne Mono and Sohne Schmal)
- Ramp (Söhne)
- Linear (Inter)
- Vercel (Inter, then custom Geist)
- Notion (Söhne)

### Pairing notes

Neo-grotesque sans pairs well with serif typefaces of similar weight class (both transitional and old-style serifs work), creating a clean modern-classical contrast for editorial systems. Avoid pairing neo-grotesque with geometric sans or humanist sans; the registers are too close and the pairing reads as indecisive. Custom-drawn neo-grotesque (Söhne, Geist) pairs especially well with stencil display faces, slab serifs, or condensed display sans for brand-system contrast.

### Common pitfalls

- The interchangeability risk is highest in this register. Neo-grotesque B2B SaaS wordmarks blur together. The brand that uses neo-grotesque without custom letterforms or strong supporting elements risks becoming visually identical to its category.
- The Helvetica problem. Helvetica is so over-used and so neutral that it now reads as "we did not make a typographic decision". Modern alternatives (Inter, Söhne, GT America) carry similar discipline with more current detailing.
- The screen-versus-print problem. Some neo-grotesque cuts are optimized for screen and look thin at print sizes; others are optimized for print and look chunky at small screen sizes. Pick based on primary application.

## Transitional serif

Modern serifs with high contrast (thick stems, thin transitions, sharp brackets). Source Serif, Charter, Lyon, IBM Plex Serif, Mrs Eaves, Caslon Pro at smaller sizes. Transitional serif sits between old-style serif (low contrast, organic) and modern Didone serif (extreme contrast, geometric).

Transitional serif reads editorial, considered, intellectual. It carries the long-form heritage of book typography while reading as contemporary. Excellent for legal, publishing, financial, and editorial brands that want serif gravity without the heritage signal of old-style serif.

Source Serif (Frank Grießhammer, 2014) is Adobe's open-source transitional serif, designed for both print and screen. Charter (Matthew Carter, 1987) is the original screen-optimized transitional serif, designed for low-resolution rendering before high-DPI displays existed; still excellent. Lyon (Kai Bernau, Commercial Type) is the contemporary editorial transitional serif of choice, used by The New York Times Magazine and many editorial brands. IBM Plex Serif (Mike Abbink, IBM, 2017) is IBM's open-source corporate serif, deployed across IBM's global identity.

### Categories that default to transitional serif

- Modern legal firms wanting serif gravity without old-fashioned positioning
- Editorial brands and publications (NYT Magazine, The Atlantic in some treatments, Cereal magazine)
- Financial services at the considered end
- Academic publishing (university presses, scholarly journals)
- Modern luxury at the editorial end (not the heritage-luxury end)

### Example brands

- The Atlantic (custom transitional serif, derived from Mrs Eaves and Filosofia)
- Vox Media (Lyon)
- Apple's editorial type (custom New York, transitional serif)
- IBM's identity system (Plex Serif)

### Pairing notes

Transitional serif pairs well with neo-grotesque sans (the modern-classical contrast is clean and editorial) and with humanist sans (both share calligraphic origins). The classic pairing for editorial is transitional serif for headlines and body, neo-grotesque sans for captions and metadata. Avoid pairing transitional serif with old-style serif; the readers will register the contrast as a mistake rather than an intention unless the deployment is deliberate.

### Common pitfalls

- The "magazine logo" trap. Transitional serif is so coded as editorial that using it for non-editorial brands can feel like the brand is trying to wear an editorial costume.
- The screen-rendering problem. Transitional serif's thin strokes can break at small screen sizes if the typeface wasn't optimized for low-DPI rendering. Pick a screen-optimized cut (Charter, Source Serif, Plex Serif) or set the wordmark large enough that the thin strokes survive.
- The "trying too hard" risk. Transitional serif communicates considered restraint; over-styling (italic, weight contrast, custom ligatures) can flip the register to ornate and undermine the restraint signal.

## Old-style serif

Classical serifs with low contrast and warm, organic shapes. Garamond, Caslon, Sabon, Adobe Garamond, Bembo, Janson Text. The letterforms reference 16th-18th century book typography. Diagonal stress, bracketed serifs, gentle thick-thin variation.

Old-style serif reads heritage, traditional, institutional. It carries weight that newer registers cannot. Used in legal firms whose history runs to a partner's name from 1923, in academic institutions that predate the country, in luxury brands whose claim is centuries-long craft. Used poorly, it reads as a fake heritage costume.

Garamond (Claude Garamond, mid-1500s) is the prototype; Adobe Garamond (Robert Slimbach, 1989) is the most-deployed modern revival. Caslon (William Caslon, 1722) is the British equivalent, long called "the safe choice for legal documents". Sabon (Jan Tschichold, 1967) is a modernized Garamond designed for industrial typesetting. Bembo (Stanley Morison's revival of Aldus Manutius's 1495 type) is the Italian Renaissance reference. All four predate every other typeface in this file by centuries.

### Categories that default to old-style serif

- Legal firms with heritage positioning (white-shoe firms, partnerships founded before 1950)
- Academic institutions (universities, presses, scholarly journals)
- Heritage luxury brands (centuries-old jewelers, watchmakers, fashion houses)
- Editorial brands with literary positioning (literary magazines, book publishers)
- Whiskey, wine, and other categories where "we have been doing this for generations" is the marketing claim

### Example brands

- The Economist (custom Caslon-derived display)
- The New Yorker (custom Caslon-derived masthead and ITC New Baskerville for body)
- Most university press identities (Yale, Oxford, Princeton)
- Most heritage law firm identities (Wachtell, Cravath, Latham use serifs derived from this register)

### Pairing notes

Old-style serif pairs well with humanist sans (both share calligraphic origins) and with neo-grotesque sans for editorial systems wanting modern-classical contrast. Avoid pairing old-style serif with geometric sans; the registers fight each other (geometric coldness vs old-style warmth). Avoid pairing old-style serif with transitional serif; the contrast is too subtle to register as intentional.

### Common pitfalls

- The dated risk. Old-style serif used without deliberate restraint reads as old-fashioned, which can be the goal (heritage) or the failure (trying to look serious by using old-looking type).
- The thin-stroke screen problem. Old-style serif's hairline serifs and thin strokes can disappear at small screen sizes. Either pick a screen-optimized old-style cut (Adobe Garamond Pro, EB Garamond) or accept that the wordmark must be set large.
- The fake-heritage risk. Using Garamond on a brand founded last year reads as costume, not heritage. The audience is not fooled by typography alone; the rest of the brand system has to carry the heritage claim.

## Slab serif

Heavy structural serifs with strong thick-thin uniformity. The serifs are blocky, rectangular, often nearly equal in weight to the stems. Sentinel, Adelle, Roboto Slab, Tisa, Rockwell, Archer, Vitesse. The visual register is strong, declarative, sometimes journalistic.

Slab serif reads strong, structural, declarative. It carries authority without the heritage baggage of old-style serif. Common in editorial work, sports brands, journalistic identities, and brands that want serif weight without the ornament of transitional or old-style serifs.

Sentinel (Hoefler & Frere-Jones, 2009) is the modern editorial slab. Adelle (TypeTogether, 2009) is the screen-optimized slab serif of choice for digital editorial. Roboto Slab is Google's open-source slab. Rockwell (Frank Hinman Pierpont, 1934) is the British slab serif of railway and industrial heritage. Tisa (Mitja Miklavčič) is the modern slab with humanist warming, used in editorial systems.

### Categories that default to slab serif

- Editorial and publishing (when the brand wants more weight than transitional serif provides)
- Sports brands (the typographic equivalent of athletic gravity)
- Journalistic identities (some newspapers, news magazines)
- Industrial and engineering brands (the structural quality reads as "we make real things")
- Outdoor and adventure brands (Filson, Patagonia in some treatments, REI's wordmark)

### Example brands

- Mailchimp's wordmark (custom slab-derived script)
- The Wall Street Journal (in older treatments)
- Various sports league identities (NFL teams, college athletics)
- Filson's wordmark (custom slab serif)

### Pairing notes

Slab serif pairs well with neo-grotesque sans (the structural similarity reads as one design language at different ornament levels) and with geometric sans (both are constructed registers). Avoid pairing slab serif with old-style serif; the contrast between structural and organic serifs reads as system inconsistency.

### Common pitfalls

- The "newspaper from 1985" risk. Some slab serifs read as 1980s editorial unless paired with current typographic systems.
- The clunky risk. Slab serifs' structural weight can feel heavy at large sizes; the wordmark needs to be sized carefully so the slabs do not overpower the letterforms.
- The genre-trapping risk. Slab serifs are so coded for sports, journalism, or industrial that using them outside those categories can feel like the brand is borrowing the register's gravity rather than earning its own.

## Display custom

Custom-drawn or heavily-modified typeface designed specifically for the brand. Reads distinctive but expensive in time and budget. The wordmark uses letterforms that exist nowhere else.

Custom display is the highest distinctiveness tier. Stripe's wordmark, Google's Product Sans, Pinterest's custom geometric, Spotify's Circular variant, Airbnb's Cereal. Each is a custom typeface drawn to solve a specific problem in the brand's wordmark or identity system.

The justification is when the brand needs a wordmark no one else has and the budget supports drawing one. Custom display is rarely justified for new brands without significant marketing investment because the brand has not yet earned the recognition that makes the custom letterforms register as distinctive. For mature brands, custom display is the ceiling of typographic discipline.

### Categories where custom display fits

- Mature brands at scale where custom letterforms become a competitive moat
- Tech-led brands with budget for type design partnerships
- Heritage luxury brands whose wordmark must be unique (not Garamond plus a name)
- Editorial brands with recognizable masthead type (The New York Times, The New Yorker)
- Sports leagues and major sports brands

### Example brands

- Stripe (custom Sohne, then Söhne plus custom optical adjustments on the wordmark)
- Google (Product Sans, custom geometric)
- Spotify (Circular variant, then custom Spotify Mix)
- Airbnb (Cereal, custom geometric humanist by Dalton Maag)
- Pinterest (custom geometric for the wordmark)

### Pairing notes

Custom display is rarely paired in lockup; the custom typeface IS the wordmark. The pairing decision is which body and metadata typefaces support the custom display in larger system applications. The body typeface is typically chosen for legibility at small sizes (a screen-optimized neo-grotesque or humanist sans) rather than for visual harmony with the custom display.

### Common pitfalls

- The cost risk. Custom letterform drawing is expensive (5-figure to 6-figure engagements depending on scope). The investment must match the brand's recognition trajectory.
- The over-customization risk. Custom letterforms should solve specific problems, not announce themselves. The custom "g" that reads as cleverly drawn rather than as just-the-letter-g signals committee work.
- The aging risk. Custom display typefaces age with the era they were drawn in. The Spotify wordmark from the 2010s reads as 2010s; the brand has had to update the system multiple times. Plan for redrawing every 5-10 years.

## How to choose, in summary

The choice flows from category default and positioning intention.

- **In-category choice signals competence.** Legal in old-style serif, SaaS in neo-grotesque sans, sports in slab serif. The audience reads "this brand understands the category".
- **Out-of-category choice signals positioning.** Legal in geometric sans signals "we are modern". SaaS in old-style serif signals "we take the work seriously". Outdoor in geometric sans signals "we are not your grandfather's outdoor brand". The shift is intentional and the rest of the brand system has to support it.
- **Custom display is reserved for mature brands** with recognition that earns the investment.
- **Pairing logic** combines the wordmark register with body and metadata registers to build the system. The pairing should read as one design intention, not as two competing ones.

The wrong typographic register is not always obvious in isolation. It becomes obvious in deployment: the wordmark on the business card, the lockup at retail, the favicon at 16px. Test the chosen register against the brand's actual application contexts before committing.
