# Linkable Assets Guide

Categories of linkable assets, with examples and effort estimates. A linkable asset is content (or a tool, or a dataset) that other sites have a reason to link to. Without a linkable asset, link building is begging. With one, it is offering value.

---

## What makes an asset linkable

A linkable asset has at least one of these properties:

- **It is the only place to find this thing.** Original data, original tool, original framework.
- **It is the best place to find this thing.** Most comprehensive, most current, most carefully built.
- **It saves the linker time.** A reusable resource the linker would otherwise have to build or explain.
- **It carries authority.** Survey from a recognized brand, published research, expert-authored.
- **It is current.** Updated more recently than alternatives, with the year visible.

Pages that fail all five are not linkable. They might be useful, but they are not assets that earn links.

## Category 1: original research

Surveys, studies, or data analyses you ran and nobody else has.

### Examples

- Annual industry survey (e.g., "State of [category] 2026")
- Benchmark report from your own product data ("the average [metric] across 1,000 companies using us")
- Trend analysis from public data sources you cleaned and synthesized
- Salary or compensation report for your industry
- Pricing transparency report ("what [category] really costs")

### Effort

- **Low end:** 40-80 hours (existing data, in-house analysis, single-author write-up)
- **High end:** 200-400 hours (custom survey, n=1,000+, design and PR investment)

### Link earning rate

Highest of any category, when done well. A defensible original research asset can earn 100+ referring domains over its life.

### When to invest

If you have unique access to data (your product's data, your customer base, your industry network) and you can update annually, this is the highest-leverage asset category.

### When to skip

If the data is shallow, the sample is small, or the analysis is obviously self-serving, the asset will be ignored or worse, criticized publicly.

## Category 2: free tools

Calculators, validators, generators, viewers - small interactive utilities that solve a specific problem.

### Examples

- Calculators (mortgage, ROI, paycheck, conversion rate)
- Validators (HTML, schema markup, robots.txt, regex)
- Generators (favicon, color palette, password, lorem ipsum, og image)
- Converters (currency, units, file format)
- Viewers (JSON, EXIF, certificate)

### Effort

- **Low end:** 20-40 hours (single-purpose calculator, basic UI)
- **High end:** 200+ hours (rich tool with saved state, account integration, PWA, accessibility-perfect)

### Link earning rate

High and durable. Tools earn links steadily for years if they remain useful and current.

### When to invest

When the problem is recurring, you can build something cleaner than what exists, and the tool naturally surfaces your product as the next step (e.g., a "how much will this site weigh" tool from a perf optimization vendor).

### When to skip

When the space is saturated with free tools that already work well. A new "color picker" is unlikely to displace the entrenched ones.

---

## Category 3: definitive guides

Long-form, exhaustive coverage of a topic that becomes the reference everyone links to.

### Examples

- "The complete guide to DMARC"
- "The HTTP status code reference"
- "Web performance metrics explained"
- "The 2026 GDPR compliance checklist"
- "All the CSS units, with examples"

### Effort

- **Low end:** 40-80 hours
- **High end:** 200+ hours, plus annual maintenance

### Link earning rate

High when the topic has natural inbound demand and the asset is genuinely the best version. Lower when 5+ competitor guides already exist.

### When to invest

When you can credibly produce something better than the current best (more comprehensive, more current, better organized, better examples, better visuals). When the topic supports your business strategically.

### When to skip

When the topic is dominated by an evergreen entrenched guide (Wikipedia-level reference, MDN, NN/g) that you cannot displace.

---

## Category 4: visual / interactive assets

Infographics, interactive explainers, micro-sites built around a single concept.

### Examples

- Interactive timelines
- Animated explainers ("how does HTTPS work")
- Map-based visualizations
- Comparison interactive ("the size of every JavaScript framework")
- Single-purpose explainer micro-sites (e.g., "supply chain visualizer")

### Effort

- **Low end:** 40-80 hours (static infographic, simple interactive)
- **High end:** 400+ hours (complex interactive, custom illustration, browser-based simulation)

### Link earning rate

High when the visual is genuinely useful and shareable. Often peaks early and decays unless the topic has lasting interest.

### When to invest

When visual treatment unlocks something prose cannot show. When the topic has strong visual storytelling potential. When you have design capacity to do it well.

### When to skip

When the same value can be delivered as a normal article. Visual treatment is not a substitute for substance; it is a multiplier on substance that already exists.

---

## Category 5: templates and frameworks

Reusable structures the audience can copy, adapt, and use.

### Examples

- Project plan templates
- Notion / Airtable templates with download
- Email outreach templates
- Pitch deck templates
- Resume / CV templates
- Code starter templates (CodeSandbox, Stackblitz, GitHub repos)
- Frameworks ("the SPADE framework for decisions")

### Effort

- **Low end:** 8-20 hours per template
- **High end:** 40-80 hours for a polished template suite

### Link earning rate

Moderate. Templates earn references in roundups and resource lists. Less likely to earn high-authority editorial links.

### When to invest

When the template solves a frequent problem in your audience's work and naturally connects to your product.

### When to skip

When the template space is crowded with free, polished alternatives.

---

## Category 6: glossaries and reference indexes

Comprehensive, well-organized indexes of terms, codes, status messages, error codes, etc.

### Examples

- "All HTTP status codes with examples"
- "Glossary of marketing terms"
- "Every accessibility WCAG criterion explained"
- "The full list of CSS pseudo-classes"

### Effort

- **Low end:** 40-80 hours
- **High end:** 200+ hours, plus continuous updates

### Link earning rate

Moderate to high. Reference assets earn links steadily as authors writing on related topics need a place to link the term.

### When to invest

When you have the patience to be exhaustive and the topic supports it.

### When to skip

When canonical references already exist and dominate (MDN, W3C, official docs).

---

## Category 7: industry rankings and lists

Annual or continuous lists ranking companies, products, or trends in a category.

### Examples

- "The 100 fastest-growing [category] companies"
- "The 50 best [job role] to follow"
- "[Industry] state-of-the-art software comparison"

### Effort

- **Low end:** 60-100 hours
- **High end:** 200+ hours with PR

### Link earning rate

Moderate to high. Ranked companies link to the list as a credibility signal. Higher rankings earn more links.

### When to invest

When you have the credibility to be the ranker (your audience trusts your judgment in the category).

### When to skip

When you would be ranking yourself or competitors with no objectivity.

---

## Category 8: events and community assets

Conferences, summits, awards, communities you sponsor or run.

### Examples

- Industry conference recap and highlight reels
- Annual awards
- Slack / Discord community
- Recurring meetup
- Podcast

### Effort

- **High.** Events and communities require continuous investment, not one-time.

### Link earning rate

Moderate. Earns links from participants, partners, press coverage.

### When to invest

When the audience-building flywheel is part of your strategy and you can sustain investment for 2+ years.

### When to skip

When the team cannot commit to sustained investment.

---

## Category 9: opinionated takes and POVs

Strong, well-argued positions on industry questions. Not lists, not data; arguments.

### Examples

- "Why we stopped using [framework]"
- "The case against [common practice]"
- "[Industry standard] is wrong, and here is why"
- Manifestos

### Effort

- **Low.** A great POV piece can be 1,500-3,000 words and 10-30 hours.

### Link earning rate

Highly variable. A POV that hits a nerve in the industry can earn 50+ links in a week. Most POVs earn nothing.

### When to invest

When the author has standing to take the position credibly (track record, role, prior writing). When the POV is genuinely held and well-defended.

### When to skip

When the POV is contrarian for the sake of being contrarian. Bad-faith hot takes earn ire and short-term traffic, not durable links.

## How to pick

Score candidates against four dimensions:

| Dimension | Weight | Score 1-5 |
|---|---|---|
| Strategic fit | 30% | Does it serve a topic cluster you want to own? |
| Defensibility | 25% | Can you build something competitors will not easily match? |
| Effort | 25% | What does it cost to ship and maintain? (Lower effort = higher score) |
| Audience pull | 20% | Will the right people link to it? |

Build the highest-scoring candidate first. Ship one asset all the way through promotion before starting the next. Two well-promoted assets beat ten orphaned ones.

---

## Promotion plan (a separate but linked task)

A linkable asset that nobody knows about earns no links. The promotion plan must exist before the build starts.

Typical promotion plan elements:

- 5-10 pre-launch outreach contacts (industry voices, friendly press, peer companies who would benefit)
- Coordinated social, email, and content launch
- Explicit "ask" templates for outreach (no link begging; offer the asset as relevant, let the linker decide)
- Follow-up content that references the asset for the next 3-6 months
- Ongoing maintenance plan (annual update for research, evergreen updates for tools)

Without a promotion plan, even great assets sit unlinked.

---

## Common failure modes

- **Asset is too thin.** Calling a 600-word article a "definitive guide" earns no respect.
- **Asset is dated.** "2022 industry report" still on the site in 2026. Looks abandoned.
- **No promotion plan.** Built, shipped, never told anyone.
- **Self-serving disguised as research.** Survey questions written to produce a result that flatters your product.
- **Tool that breaks.** Calculator with stale data, validator that misses common cases. Returns negative trust.
- **Asset that offers no follow-up.** Visitor arrives, uses it, leaves. No reason to subscribe, register, or convert.

## Rough effort vs durable links matrix

| Category | Effort | Typical referring domains over asset's life |
|---|---|---|
| Free tool (single-purpose) | 30 hours | 40-200 |
| Free tool (rich) | 200 hours | 100-1,000 |
| Original research (annual) | 100 hours | 50-500 per edition |
| Definitive guide | 80 hours | 30-300 |
| Visual / interactive | 80 hours | 30-200 |
| Templates suite | 40 hours | 10-100 |
| Glossary / reference index | 80 hours | 50-300 |
| Industry ranking (annual) | 100 hours | 50-300 per edition |
| Community / event | 1,000+ hours | 100-1,000 |
| POV piece | 20 hours | 0-200 (high variance) |

These are rough ranges from typical SaaS and content-led businesses. Industry, audience size, and promotion quality all shift them substantially.
